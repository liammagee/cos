Executive Summary

Six urban pilot projects launched in 2008-09 have formed the core of a World Vision evidence base for urban programming innovation and approaches. The pilots are based on principles of action- learning and operational field research - ‘letting theory emerge from practice.’ The ‘meta analysis’ of urban pilot projects commenced in FY13 as a priority of the Centre of Expertise for Urban Programming (Urban CoE). The purpose of this exercise is to formalise and share what the Urban CoE has learned about working in urban settings, compare this emerging knowledge across diverse urban contexts, and determine implications for programming and funding models.

The pilot project in South Africa, which began implementation in September 2010 and exists now in the second project phase as the Khuthala Orlando East Urban Project (FY11-14), was the first of the pilots to be reviewed as part of the meta analysis process. This report details the findings of the review exercise, conducted in November 2012.

Methodology

The starting point for the review methodology was the Urban Meta Framework (UMF). The framework was developed at the beginning of the pilot process, and consists of key urban themes, questions and indicators to be explored in the six country contexts. The UMF was used to inform baseline studies in the pilots (including Orlando East), thus providing data for comparison at the time of review.

The UMF was tested as an evaluative framework for the first time in Orlando East. The urban themes therein were translated into a set of indicators and instrument questions in three surveys targeting community members, project partners, and World Vision staff. A total of 27 semi-structured interviews were conducted applying these instruments, which included both qualitative and some quantitative questions. In addition to this, a quantitative ‘partnership assessment’ questionnaire based on a VicHealth tool2 was trialled in a small number of partner interviews.

Results were analysed by identifying prominent themes, using online software called Dedoose (qualitative research analysis software).

Limitations of the methodology, covered in detail in the full report, include the limited available time and small cohort size for interviews, the difficult of translating a generic framework and questions to a specific local context, the broad scope of surveys, and the variable accuracy and quality of notes.

It should be noted that it was not one of the objectives of this review to conduct a full impact evaluation of the Orlando East project. It was determined early in the planning that the project was not in a position to be evaluated halfway into the second phase of implementation, and that this would complicate the first review–primarily because it was as much a test of the UMF as an evaluative framework as it was about learning from the pilot itself. A blending of the objectives and methodology of impact evaluation with UMF-based learning will be tested in the second pilot study in Cambodia from March 2013.

Key Findings and Analysis

Key findings of the Orlando East review are grouped according to the most prominent categories of results, and linked to specific conclusions and/or recommendations.

Economic landscape of Orlando East

The review found a wide range of contextual factors influencing the project goal of ‘contributing towards local economic development through partnerships and community engagement in wards 29, 30 and 31 in the City of Johannesburg by 2014.’3 Upon analysis of the themes emerging from interviews, it became clear that these factors–though not specifically ‘economic’ issues–were nevertheless significant obstacles to the realisation of local business or employment objectives.

Drugs and alcohol, crime (‘lawlessness’), and the prevalance of HIV and AIDS were all noted as significant social challenges. Stakeholders in Orlando East were also highly critical of government, complaining variously of corruption problems, perceived inefficiencies, and a lack of transparency and accountability in the conduct of government. Perhaps not surprisingly, government-related issues are emphasised in this report as one of the primary themes affecting the economic outlook of Orlando East.

Another major gap identifed was also the community’s access to–and willingess to use–‘information.’ Examples of information in this case included employment opportunities, rules and regulations, or government plans. A related problem–the lack of community ‘spirit’ and volunteerism–was seen as not helpful in the local business environment.

Although employment options seem to be available (primarily in small business and manual labour), various factors mitigate against the takeup of these opportunities. These include lack of relevant skills, education, and the availability of finance or credit. A lack of awareness regarding financial management, banking and debt was a related issue, as was the perceived increase in ‘competition’ from foreign migrants starting up small businesses in the area.

Finally, the political history of Soweto (South Western Township) cannot be overstated as having influenced the current economic conditions in Orlando East, including the status of Soweto as essentially a dormitory suburb with restrictions on economic activity until at least 1977. Coupled with a high population (currently believed to be more than 2 million) and the ‘informality’ and ‘high mortality rates’ of local businesses, it is perhaps not surprising that community respondents felt there was a considerable way to go towards improving the broader economic outlook in Orlando East.

World Vision urban pilot project

Among those who know about World Vision and/or have an active partnership with the Orlando East project, World Vision is held in high regard as a reliable, consultative partner. The project’s efforts in connecting people to the job market and facilitating connections between organisations have been well appreciated by interview respondents. Both the activities and physical presence of World Vision in the area were considered visible by respondents.

The project has clearly been effective in communicating the changed focus of World Vision in Orlando East to ‘economic development’. The World Vision ADP previously active in the area included more direct community support activities, such as the distribution of food parcels, and so we note that it would have been no small challenge to make a transition to a short term project based on facilitation, sharing information and various forms of advocacy.

The legacy of a long-term engagement with World Vision has undoubtedly contributed to the high levels of trust shared with the community. However, we note that such is the importance of World Vision in the community that most of the existing partnerships are reliant upon World Vision as a facilitator, and indeed these could not be sustained without its involvement. We were not able to discern from the interview data just who else could be a similarly trusted, neutral and connected community leader in World Vision’s place.

A network-mapping exercise conducted with the local Community Development Committee in 2009 showed government at the centre of the diagram. The role of World Vision appears to have evolved into an important intermediary and facilitator between community and government. Our analysis suggests that this position reflects an ‘interim’ stage of project maturity, where World Vision is now central to the partner network, and the capacity of partners to sustain their activities needs to be built. This highlights what could be a similar challenge for other ‘pilots’ and similar ‘entry projects’ that have limited timeframes and exit strategies at the outset.

Although the Indicator Tracking Table shows under-achieved quantitative targets for the project (e.g. number of youth trained to take up entrepreneurial and job opportunities4), it is noted in this review that the project design does not accurately reflect the substantial time involved in basic coordination of activities across a range of organisations and industries. Given time and resource constraints on the project team, it is possible that a more focused approach–working with fewer partners and less prescriptive outputs in each case–would accomplish more. This would entail a much less ambitiously scoped project design and in the process may help reduce the burden of M&E and budgeting described by staff. Alternatively, this may constitute evidence that the project is ready to be scaled up, with more staff put in place to coordinate the many partner relationships more intensively.

Partnering and networking in urban contexts

Working with and through partners wherever possible as a project approach was emphasised repeatedly by staff during the review, and multiple references to this can be found in project documents. While this is clearly a dominant theme with regard to urban engagement, what is less clear are the specifics of ‘partnering’ as an objective, an activity, a staff attribute or a measurable indicator in Orlando East. In reality it constitutes all of these elements, and there are multiple ways that partnering can be described and demonstrated.

The assertion of ‘development as partnership’, or partnering as the primary mode of operating in urban contexts, needs to be investigated further both in this pilot project and in future reviews. More detailed reflection and evidence on this theme would assist in articulating the:

  • different types of partnership in urban environments,
  • value of each ‘type’ for achieving goals or objectives, and
  • process and attributes required to develop each partnership.

Urban programming approaches

As described, one of the drivers of the meta analysis process is the need to apply pilot project lessons to current thinking on urban programming and funding approaches in World Vision. The Orlando East review highlights some key lessons on programming that should be compared with future reviews.

One of the key findings was that the most practical way to effect transformation in Orlando East is to invest in ‘soft’ infrastructure, including facilitation, partnering, networking and advocacy as previously described. This imperative makes it difficult to describe project activities or demonstrate evidence of progress in any tangible way: “Sorry we don’t have a vegetable garden.” Project staff run meetings, participate in public gatherings, provide advice to stakeholders, and make referrals to service providers. These daily activities are admittedly difficult to measure, yet are in fact conducted with a high degree of sensitivity to stakeholder interests, political undertones, and ethical considerations such as equity and inclusion. In addition, relationships with community and partners need to be managed with maturity and transparency in order to be effective.

These intangible qualities should inform World Vision’s role in contributing to developmental outcomes and child well-being in urban contexts. Indeed, if it were possible to build enough support and ownership of child well-being aspirations among local partners, the urban approach of Orlando East would be ideal for establishing a longer-term sustainability and transition plan. By this we mean that World Vision would already be playing an indirect facilitation role, and the accountability for child well-being would already sit with communities, partners, and government. This is in contrast to World Vision having to initially play a direct role in bringing about these outcomes, with a need to transition this role over time.

Project management, and Design, Monitoring & Evaluation (DM&E)

Several important lessons for the development and management of urban projects emerged through exploration of UMF themes during this review.

The review team began to surmise that the word ‘project’ was not an entirely accurate way to describe the nature of the engagement in Orlando East. This is because conventional methods of ‘project management’, such as results or outcome-based accountability approaches (logframes, etc.) were only partially applicable, and tend to under-emphasise or, at worst, mask actual activities and achievements on the ground. While the fiscal accountability elements of logframes, for instance, were appreciated, the usefulness of these tools for demonstrating progress, or even measuring relevant indicators, were thrown into question.

One example of the ‘misfit’ of conventional project management approaches in urban contexts is the lack of meaningful qualitative data collected since the 2010 baseline study. The baseline methodology included the collection of in-depth case studies on individuals within Orlando East, as a way of representing a ‘snapshot’ of personal livelihood situations and outlooks. The intention of this approach was to establish a pseudo-longitudinal study of these individuals, such that:

The experiences of the following people will be tracked throughout the life of the project to develop an understanding of the changes that will take place and the impacts of the project5

However, this approach does not appear in the M&E plan for the original Orlando East project, and therefore was not included within implementation plans or indicator tracking tables in subsequent years.

While the project team tried in earnest to track down these individuals to include them in the review cohort, in most cases they were unavailale or no longer living in the area. Had this review been structured as a formal evaluation, it would have been difficult to use the baseline case studies as anything but indicative ‘snapshots’ of life for certain individuals in Orlando East. This highlights the limitations of standardised indicator tracking and reporting mechanisms within ‘projects’ that need to be reconsidered in light of the different requirements in urban settings.

The original project design suggests that:

It is also important to realize that M&E is not necessarily based on the indicators but also on the definitive features of an urban setting. Research and learning through regular reflection sessions will also be performed.6

Despite this, interviews with staff revealed that learning and reflection opportunities–and particularly the documentation of these lessons–were now missing from project implementation. One possible explanation is the absence of a DM&E support staff or team member who could facilitate reflection activities. The current staff cohort of only 2 are unable to do this on their own:

When you are involved in the game, you cannot be the referee. You need someone to stop the game and talk about the rules.

Finally, the review raised a number of issues related to financial management and finance systems, particularly in obtaining approval for funding the sorts of activities that accompany facilitation, partnering and advocaacy. Staff would likely operate more effectively with a more flexible accounting regime, provided that funded activities could be reconciled to project outcomes.

Key recommendations for Orlando East project

A summary of recommendations made by the review team, in light of the findings and analysis, is provided below. We suggest some possible areas to be tabled with relevant World Vision South Africa staff, in order to inform future plans for the Orlando East project–particularly to address planning for sustainability and transition, or, alternatively, project ‘scale up’:

  1. A more targeted and strategic approach to partnering. Some of the more established project partners, such as the SMME Forum and PlanAct, are more directly involved in economic development activities, and also have strong organisational capacities for facilitating this. Project outputs could be simplified to focus on what can be achieved through stronger–and possibly financial–partnerships with these kinds of organisations.
  2. Related to the above, we suggest possibly separating general World Vision community assistance from its economic development work. Current staff seemed adept at separating these activities (and as we note, in practice they are closely related in this context). However they do not seem to be formally acknowledged, and complicate the project design.
  3. As we note, economic development is a highly complex field, and even more so given the other challenges in this environment. We recommend that operational staff be supplemented by research capacity in the areas of business, finance and economics – possibly through internships from the University of Johannesburg. This could assist in identifying particular opportunities for business and employment in the area.
  4. The aim of creating a self-sustaining partner network in the area is, in our view, currently unrealistic. We suggest this either means extending the life of the pilot considerably, or examining mechanisms, such as a consultancy or small non-government organisation, to take up the work currently undertaken by World Vision. The latter option is unlikely to be productive without at least some kind of ongoing support from World Vision itself.
  5. The link between economic development and child wellbeing is difficult to realise, and to measure, in any kind of 3-5 year project timeframe. This poses the question of whether a longer term plan needs to be defined, at least schematically. Such a plan could include transitional arrangements, where specific World Vision-funded development projects could ‘spin-off’ to other entities. A current difficulty is understanding who those entities might be in this area.
  6. Staff expressed concerns about the time and complexity of current processes for reporting and budgeting. Given the nature of the work on this project, these concerns appeared well founded. We recommend finding ways to allow for more flexible allocation of funds to support ‘soft’ development work involved in partnering, facilitation and advocacy (covering various areas such as venue hire, catering, transportation, communication, printing and so on).
  7. Associated with the previous recommendation, it may be that current project management tools need to be modified to fit the purpose and mode of engagement in the community. Moving forward, a key contribution of this pilot could be the trialing of differential approaches to M&E/financial management that reflect the elements of facilitation, negotiation, relationship-building, and other associated activities.

Recommendations for future reviews/pilot evaluations

We make the following recommendations for the methodological aspects of future reviews:

  1. Continue with iterations of the current community, partner and staff instruments (including the Partner Assessment Tool).
  2. Incorporate formal instructions for interviewers, plain language statements and consent forms.
  3. Continue with the practice of ‘role-playing’ interviews on the first day of the review.
  4. Record interviews wherever possible.
  5. Separate ‘quant’ from ‘qual’ survey instruments, and conduct the quantitative surveys separately, with larger cohorts.
  6. After initial analysis of the review or evaluation data, administer supplementary surveys with proejct staff or select partners to elicit further information. This could particularly useful for emergent themes that are unexpected, or for unanswered questions/extra detail that may be required to complete the analysis.
  7. Terms like “community”, “government”, “partnership” and “project” should be anchored at the outset of the interview (as well as clarified as much as possible at the start of the review process). In practice we often clarified these terms, but not always in consistent ways. A recommendation would be to have: A specific set of questions that asks the respondents to name their “community”, their interaction with “government”, the nature of their “partnership” with World Vision, and their knowledge of and interaction with the “project”.
  8. Include additional methods where appropriate, including:
    • Network mapping
    • Semi-structured observation (using “Walk&Talk” or similar approaches)
    • Analysis of secondary materials, including published literature, statistical sources and urban cultural forms
  9. Continue to examine methods for measuring impact and effectiveness of partnering, facilitation and advocacy. One specific recommendation here is to develop an indicator of partner network ‘maturity’–the extent to which a set of partnerships could survive without one or more participants.

Introduction

The Orlando East review described in this report was conducted in the last week of November, 2012. Two World Vision Australia staff, John van Kooy and David Lansley, were accompanied to Johannesburg by Liam Magee, an academic from RMIT University acting as a consultant. We spent a total of 5 days in Orlando East. On the Monday we met the World Vision staff at head office, followed by introductions to the Orlando East project team and students from the nearby University of Johannesburg. We also rehearsed and refined the interview scripts with the students. Tuesday to Thursday we conducted interviews with a range of community members and partners, mostly in the Orlando East area. On Friday we conducted 4 staff interviews in the morning, and spent the afternoon writing up remaining notes from the interviews.

The Orlando East review is the first of six planned reviews of urban pilot projects by World Vision’s Centre of Expertise for Urban Programming. As such, it also pilots an approach to evaluating project progress in urban contexts. For reasons we outline in this report, the urban locus of these projects provides both challenges and opportunities for evaluation. This review dwells at some length on these. In addition to the discussion of the Orlando East project itself, we reflect here upon broader theoretical and methodological questions concerning evaluation in the urban context.

The Orlando East Project

The Orlando East Project is well described in the Baseline Project (World Vision South Africa 2010a):

The Orlando East Urban Pilot Project is one of the projects of the Urban Programs Initiative (UPI) which was set up by World Vision Australia to research and develop programming approaches suited for the urban context.

Situated in Soweto, Johannesburg, the Orlando East urban pilot project was initiated by the UPI during the redesign phase of the Orlando East ADP. The ADP had been operating for ten years and was based on a direct benefits model of programming.

The Orlando East Urban Pilot project began in 2008 with a particular focus in developing a Local Economic Development approach suitable for addressing issues of livelihoods in the urban context and helping to transition the existing ADP towards more sustainable forms of development programming. The Assessment and Design phase included the piloting of the Participatory Appraisal for Competitive Advantage (PACA) tool which resulted in a number of priority areas identified and task teams generated to promote local economic development within Orlando East. The task teams were formed around the following areas: Environment, Small and Medium Enterprises, Skills Development, Tourism, and Informal Traders.

Through developing the capacity of the task teams and strengthening existing local governance mechanisms, the project aims to contribute towards improved economic conditions in Orlando East and ultimately enhancing the quality of life for children in Orlando East.

Since the Baseline Report, there have been several minor changes of focus. As the 2012 Project Design outlines (World Vision South Africa 2012), there has been a greater emphasis more recently on partnering (with other community and business organisations), advocacy (to government) and facilitation (between community, business and government groups). Accordingly, there has been less focus on World Vision itself driving economic development activities (through training, workshops and other initiatives).

Another change is the welcome recognition of the interplay between economic development and child wellbeing, on the one hand, and a host of what have been termed ‘cross cutting themes’, on the other. Such themes include health (specifically HIV/AIDS), drugs and crime, government accountability, child abuse and inadequate skills and information. Our findings confirm these are major obstacles to both economic development and child wellbeing.

Background

Soweto Economic Overview

Soweto, an acronym for South Western Township, is a predominantly black urban area within the municipality of Johannesburg in Gauteng Province. It lies 23 km south of Johannesburg, and covers an area of 106 sq km. Soweto arose out of a cluster of townships created to house black miners and other workers. The 1923 Native (Urban Areas) Act allowed the forced relocation of black people, and by 1936, 12,000 people had been relocated to Orlando East, an early Soweto township. Forced removals to Soweto accelerated after the start of apartheid in 19489, and migration to Soweto has continued in the post-apartheid era. Soweto is now the largest black urban area in South Africa.

Soweto’s population has risen substantially over the past decade. The 2001 census counted 858,644 people; by 2008 the population was estimated to be 1.3 million and is currently believed to be more than 2 million10. Growth in Soweto’s population is consistent with trends in Gauteng Province as a whole. Between 2001 and 2011, Gauteng’s share of South Africa’s population rose from 21.04% to 22.39%, and the province attracted a net inflow of 367,076 people over the five years to 2011. Only one other province, Western Cape, had a significant inflow of people over that period (95,556)11. At the 2001 census, population density was around 8,100 people per square km, and is almost certainly higher now given the growth of prefabricated housing in the yards of existing dwellings.

On these numbers, Soweto’s population density is relatively high when compared with other UPI pilot project sites, for example:

  • Surabaya (2012) - 8,300/km2
  • La Paz (2008) - 1,861.2/km2
  • Kanpur (2011) - 1,366/km2

However, with the majority of housing being single and two-storied dwellings, it is still not especially high by global city standards.

Income

Soweto is frequently referred to as a low income area, although the spread of income (or perhaps more accurately wealth) is wide (including allegedly 50 millionaires resident in Soweto).

Income Ranges by Province (2004)

Data in Figure 112 confirms the north region (which includes Orlando East) is one of the poorer parts of Soweto. The proportion of households in the three lowest income categories was 43.6% in the north, similar to the east’s 44.0%, and well above the south’s 34.7%. The north and east both had the highest proportion of households with ‘limited and erratic income’ (25.2%), again well above the south’s 15.9%. Per capita income per annum in 2004 however, was lowest in the central area of Soweto. At R9,381 it was nearly 7% below the north’s level of R10,068, nearly 14% below the east’s R10,875, and more than 32% below the South’s R13,8758.

Housing

Soweto suffers from a chronic shortage of housing. The movement of people to Soweto –- both forced, and more recently, to take advantage of employment opportunities in the Johannesburg area–has meant that housing has been in short supply for many years13. Original four room ‘matchbox’ houses dating back to before the Second World War remain an important source of housing in the older parts of Soweto, including Orlando, though many have been since improved and extended. There are also significant areas of informal settlement14. The introduction of a 99 year leasehold system in 1978 encouraged private home ownership among wealthier Soweto residents, and fostered privately developed housing estates and infill housing, including in Orlando West and the various Proteas. A collapse in the property market in the early 1990s saw banks largely withdraw from home lending. In the post- apartheid era the government has played a larger role in the provision of housing, but supply has failed to keep up with demand, and a significant backlog remains15. Basic infrastructure, including paved roads, water16 and sewerage is also limited in parts of Soweto.

The 2004 Soweto Market Research Survey collected data on the Soweto housing stock by broad area. Orlando East is in the north region. The data in Figure 217 below show that

  • Home ownership–outright and mortgage–is 47%. For comparison homeownership in Spain, Italy and Belgium is 78%, Brazil (another BRICS economy) 74%, Britain and Australia 69%, the US 67%, and Germany 42%18. The relatively low level of home ownership in Soweto is probably at least partly due to restrictions on property ownership by black people for many years, plus low incomes.
  • Brick houses are the most common type of dwellings in Soweto, accounting for 58% of the total.
  • Informal dwellings - house/flat in backyard, informal unit in backyard, informal unit not in backyard–are important, accounting for 35% of total dwellings.

Economy

The economy of Soweto has been shaped considerably by its history. The 1923 Native (Urban Areas) Act, as well as legalising forced relocation, imposed restrictions on the employment of African people unless approved by the local authority. Subsequent legislation, including the 1957 Natives (Urban Areas) Consolidation Act limited employment in Soweto to seven categories - operating general shops, butcheries, eating houses, selling milk or vegetables, or hawking goods. These restrictions encouraged development of a substantial informal economy. While restrictions on economic activity were eased in 1977, giving rise to a local taxi industry, informal trading is still an important economic activity for some people in Soweto19.

The economy of Soweto is also influenced by its proximity to Johannesburg. Johannesburg is the biggest city in South Africa and the main financial centre of Africa. It is the base for South Africa’s five TV stations, and is claimed to have the best telecommunications and technology on the African continent. There is also a range of manufacturing in and around Johannesburg including basic iron and steel, fabricated and metal products, food, machinery, electrical machinery, appliances and electrical supplies, vehicle parts and accessories, chemical products, food processing and beverages20.

Despite its physical size and proximity to Johannesburg, the City of Johannesburg has estimated that, based on 2001 census and other data, Soweto’s formal economy made only a small contribution to the overall Johannesburg metropolitan economy21. As Figure 322 shows, while Soweto accounted for more than 40% of the region’s population, its unemployment rate was much higher than the rest of the region and its economic contribution was less than 5% of total gross value added (GVA). Moreover, Soweto’s economic contribution was heavily skewed towards the largely government funded community services sector (including clinics, schools and hospitals). The contribution of other areas of economic activity such as construction, transport and wholesale and retail trade was each in the range 3-4%23.

Soweto and Greater Johannesburg (2001)

Within Soweto, small and micro businesses are the main type of private economic activity. A study by the Bureau of Market Research at the University of South Africa of small informal businesses in Soweto over the period 2007-2011 found ‘relatively high mortality rates’24. Just under 40% of businesses were still operating at the end of the period, and survival rates were particularly low for street vendors and home-based businesses. Further, fewer businesses were started than failed, implying a net decline of almost 50% in the number of small businesses in the study area. Failing to respond to an increasingly competitive environment was seen as a major reason for business failure. Entrepreneurs who adapted to greater competitiveness, for example by changing or limiting their product range or reducing employment, were more likely to survive. Larger and incorporated businesses, those operating from permanent premises, those in older established business centres, and franchises were found to have better prospects for survival.

Methodology

The Orlando East Urban Pilot Project Review is the first of a series of such reviews for the six urban pilot projects currently underway, and is therefore also a piloting of a new methodology for conducting reviews of projects in urban environments. As such it builds upon a number of further sources to inform its methodology.

Aims of the Methodology

The methodology supports a number of aims identified at the WV Meta-analysis Workshop:

  1. Collate and draw on learnings from past and existing World Vision urban projects sourced from experienced agencies and academic institutions engaged in urban issues.

  2. Contribute to our knowledge of the diversity of urban community contexts and the specific development strengths and challenges entailed in these.

  3. Explore the relevance and applicability of a range of urban funding and programming approaches and tools that contribute to child well-being in urban environments.

  4. Identify and document organisational and management enablers and barriers for effective implementation of urban programs, including:

    1. Staffing and core competencies

    2. Staff capacity building

    3. Organisational business processes

    4. Management support

    5. Organisational systems

  5. Assess scale up potential of urban pilot approaches

Existing Sources for Methodology Design

The three main sources are:

  • Previous Theory of Change (World Vision South Africa 2010b), Baseline Report (World Vision South Africa 2010a) and Project Design (World Vision South Africa 2012) documents
  • Current (November 2012) drafts of the Urban Meta-analysis Framework and Research and Evaluation Framework (World Vision South Africa 2012) (hereafter UMF)
  • CWB and TDI Indicators, derived from the Compendium of Child Wellbeing Indicators (World Vision International 2011)

Additional sources that have been consulted include:

  • LEAP template and guidelines
  • Results of a WV Meta-analysis Workshop between WV UPI and RMIT Global Cities Research Institute staff on 3 November 2012
  • Specific sources on the Orlando East Project itself

Survey Instruments in Orlando East

Interviewees were identified as belonging to a Community, Partner or World Vision Staff group, and asked questions from different interview scripts accordingly. Additionally, questions were grouped into three categories:

  • Introductory Questions - identify and introduce the interviewee, as well as the organisation or department they represent (where relevant).

  • Framework Questions - derived from the Urban Meta-review Framework (UMF), these questions explored themes that relate to urban development generally.

  • Issue-based Questions - relate to the specific issue addressed by this pilot project. In this case, the project issue was Economic Development.

Latest versions of the interview scripts are included in the Appendix below. The Framework Questions in particular should be compared against the latest version of the Urban Meta-review Framework itself.

Analysis of Results

The survey instruments include a range of both 10-point scales and longer verbal questions. We used simple descriptive statistics to describe scale item responses and thematic coding to group qualitative responses. The coding was conducted in Dedoose, an online qualitative data analysis tool. Codes were constructed in a “bottom-up” fashion, to reflect prevalent and emergent themes from responses. Although applied in an organic fashion, some effort was made to standardise and rationalise the codes across all documents. In the case of the most relevant themes, such as partnering and economic development, a large number of second-level or sub-codes were added to discriminate responses to these issues. Within Dedoose, notes were also differentiated in terms of the groups (Community, Partner, Staff) they belonged to.

Limitations

The review suffered from a number of limitations:

  • Being the first review, the generic framework of questions and indicators (referred to as the Urban Meta-analysis Framework or UMF) were largely untested. Much of our analysis has therefore focussed on the framework itself, in addition to the specific Orlando East project. An associated limitation is that the indicators, questions and grouping themes were subject to considerable change leading upto and even during the review.

  • Interview subjects were hand-picked by World Vision staff, creating potential for biased sampling and results.

  • Small sample size and inconsistent application of questions severely curtail validity of quantitative findings.

  • The “bottom-up” approach to coding allows for considerable subjectivity in both the selection and application of codes.

  • The scope of the surveys is very broad: from specific and local questions about the project, through to general and macro-level questions about urban programming and economic development. While this was handled ‘on-the-fly’ by calibrating questions to suit particular respondents and contexts, this breadth is an issue for consistency and reliability of responses.

  • Respondents varied greatly in terms of English competency. In a number of cases we were therefore reliant upon student assistants from the local university to translate questions and responses. This limited both the accuracy and volume of responses recorded.

  • In addition to the interviewer at least one note-taker was present at all interviews. However accuracy varied due to a number of practical reasons (translation, sensitivity to context, extent of notes), and this is reflected in the final interview notes.

  • While the 2010 Baseline methodology included an in-depth case study approach targeting a range of local residents in Orlando, when designing the methodology for the Review it was found that most of the original interviewees were in available or had moved on from the area. In addition, information about these local residents had not been maintained over time and thus there was little scope for comparability between their changing situation between Baseline and Review.

Many of these limitations are offset by the ‘pilot’ nature of the review exercise itself. However they are pertinent both to further pilot reviews, and more generally, to evaluations of projects in urban contexts. We return to some of these issues in the Conclusions and Recommendations section.

Qualitative Results

Community Results

Situational Assessment

Community responses were somewhat split on question 24a, ‘Please indicate whether, over the past year, you believe your community’s situation has been improving, staying the same, or getting worse’. Two respondents thought the situation had been improving. One suggested the reason for this was that people in the community were ‘learning more things, skills’. Another directly referenced the work of World Vision in agricultural training, stating that ‘People are improving because of this training. They try to stand for themselves’. Two respondents thought things were getting worse, with one suggesting that the ‘situation was getting worse’ due to a wide range of factors: use of drugs and alcohol, crime rates, poverty and unemployment, HIV/AIDS causing parents to go into prostitution. They concluded their response with a general assessment of desparation, saying the community was characterised by ‘lawlessness’ and that children ‘no longer have respect for elders’. Four other respondents thought the situation remained the same, with one respondent also suggesting that factors of drug use, poverty and crime remained endemic.

Vulnerability

Reponses to question 25a, ‘Please identify the main causes of community vulnerability’, generally referenced similar factors: unemployment, poverty, HIV/AIDS, drugs and crime. One respondent also referenced problems of ‘crowded and poor quality housing, and the influx of people to Orlando’. The issue of age demography arose in two other responses: one stated that it was ‘particularly difficult for young people’, while another mentioned that children were frequently left with elderly carers due to the impact of AIDS. Education, skills and access to information were mentioned by several respondents as strategies for mitigating vulnerability.

Inequality

Question 26, ‘In your community, is there a big difference between the richest and poorest members?’, sought to explore perceptions of financial inequality in the community. All but two reponses were in agreement with the implied proposition. Some suggested that as people grew wealthier they tended to move away, while one respondent said that more recently there has been a trend for the growing middle class in the region to stay and renovate their homes. Although inequality is a noted feature of both Sowetan and South African society generally, as a note for future surveys, this particular question needs to be more neutrally worded in future surveys, to reduce its inherent bias and improve validity.

Mobility

Question 27, ‘Do you think these issues influence how much people move (e.g. from rural areas to the city)?’, suggested that economic opportunity is the main movitation for mobility. One respondent stated that ‘poor people are moving to find jobs in other cities; when the “place is dry” they will go to another place’. Another mentioned that rural migration was happening because of ‘better opportunities’, though this was leading to increased racism in Noordgesig (a neighbouring district of Orlando East): the area ‘used to be a coloured area, now there are different races and its hard for them to accept’.

Local Economic Development Project

Questions 29, ‘Do you believe the World Vision project has had a significant impact on the poor in your community?’, elicited a range of reponses. Some respondents referenced specific impacts: ‘tools [for] food gardening’; ‘food parcels’; bursaries and awards to students; and more general assistance to the vulnerable. Two other respondents thought, however, the specific economic aims of the project were not well-known in the community. Only one respondent referenced specific activities, stating:

The more we make meetings with them the more we grow. Also in terms of networking with other youth members of Noordgesig and Soweto and other areas.

Attitudes towards Government, Community and World Vision

Questions 58a, 59a, 60a, 61a, 62a and 63a all relate to questions of engagement with government, community, and World Vision. Accordingly, responses to these questions are discussed in aggregate. In general respondents were critical of both government and community sectors, and positive in relation to the role played by World Vision. Of particular significance was the emphasis on the need for better sources of community information.

Attitudes towards Government

Questions 58a and 63a dealt specifically with the role of government, and it should be noted, both questions and responses tended to conflate local, provincial and national government levels. This conflation could itself be viewed as an indicator, however, of poor levels of engagement with the Orlando East community. Respondents felt government had specifically ignored the plight of the poor. In the words of one respondent:

This government of ours – they are looking for riches. They are not looking out for the poor.

Another respondent stated that ‘other people are closer with the government but not those that are at the bottom’.

Yet another demanded that the government:

Stop looking after rich people. For example, the President meets with businesses in Joburg, but not with poor people in Soweto.

Respondents also felt overwhelmingly that the government had failed to communicate effectively:

Hard for community to reach the government… Many people in the Ward forums have been complaining about that.

Another respondent linked this to a more general issue of accountability:

Government does not give feedback if they are delayed in delivering specific services. The government do not report back or do follow ups.

Even when public meetings are held, outcomes are less than optimal:

Attendance is by chance. When notices are distributed they are met with negative comments, which affects attendance.

And:

Information is available but access is not. Pamphlets handed out the day before meetings. Informed community too late. Invite them with time.

These two issues of inequity and communication were linked to the issues of corruption and nepotism. One respondent stated that ‘people who engage are those involved with political parties’, while another explicitly mentioned the issue of ‘nepotism – ANC members benefit each other’s families’. A third respondent felt that the local government did provide jobs for the community, but this was frequently linked to the ‘ongoing problem of bribery’.

Some responses indicated ways the government could do more to assist the community. One suggested that ‘there must be more programs about government issues on Soweto TV’, while another recommended that the goverment ‘must create parks’, as these can ‘make a difference’.

Against the generally critical trend, one respondent did feel the govenment had played a positive role, stating that ‘the community’s ability to engage with government was good’ and the government had ‘made itself accountable for failure to deliver on promises’.

Attitudes towards Community

Questions 59a, 60a and 61a asked respondents to provide further details on related closed questions that seek to measure attitudes towards aspects of the local community. These aspects include, for the respective question, the community’s ability to work together, its awareness of its rights as citizens, and its access to information. As a preliminary note, in hindsight the order of these questions should be reversed, to reflect the logic of decision-making and agency: how well the community is informed (61a); how well it is able to transform information into decisions (60a); and how well those decisions can be acted upon collectively (59a). Accordingly, responses are presented in this reverse order.

Information

The five respondents to this question were divided about the levels of information in the community. Three thought there was sufficient information available on community matters, but that people did not utilise it effectively, due to ‘ignorance’ and lack of motivation. In the words of one respondent:

There is a multi purpose community centre (MPCC) that is responsible for providing information to people. Library provides a lot of information, but people do not go seeking information. They are ignorant.

The other two respondents felt that while there was sufficient information, not all community members had adequate access to it. One complained that:

Pamphlets handed out the day before meetings. Informed community too late. Invite them with time.’

Rights

This translated into similar ambivalence about the community’s awareness of its rights. One respondent felt that ‘people know their rights but not their obligations’; another agreed, but suggested that they “don’t know how they can practice their rights”. Others felt again there was a general problem of ‘ignorance’, both about rights and ways to express them. Respondents were divided about whether this related to the prior problem of information, or to a lack of general enterprise and hope in the community. In the words of one respondent:

People are ignorant and reluctant to take advantage of whats being offered. They feel government is not doing anything good. People are resistant, don’t want to find out information because of ANC.

A number of respondents suggested potential remedies: more timely notification of public meetings; more widespead use of community boards and pamplets; and growing use of the Internet and social media.

As a side note, while this question was worth asking, the review team felt that a prior question needed to be asked concerning respondents’ perceptions of rights, so that the question concerning awareness would relate to reasonable and context-appropriate “rights”.

Community Action

There was similarly some variance in responses to the ability of the community to work towards common goals. Some felt there was reasonable co-operation, but that this was compromised by lack of information and empowerment. A specific issue raised by one respondent was that a number of people lacked adequate identification and could not therefore vote (clearly an issue related both to information and expression of rights). Another respondent (a small trader from Mozambique) expressed a stark lack of community co-operation:

People aren’t working together and they are looking out for themselves… Money and stereotyping are the main challenges that they are facing in the community.

A further issue concerned low rates of volunteerism, which for one respondent also limited employment options:

People do not want to volunteer. They want jobs and so sometimes they don’t volunteer… They don’t understand that volunteering can lead to getting a job – still trying to find a way.

These particular difficulties had an obvious negative impact given the importance of community co-ordination: ‘[we have] high rates of crime so we need to involve communities in these issues’.

Attitudes towards World Vision

Verbal responses to question 62a, about community participation in the World Vision project, reflected the high scores the associated quantitative question (62) received. This assessment is slightly skewed by the fact that interviewers only asked this pair of question if they felt the respondents themselves were aware of the project and World Vision’s role in it.

All respondents felt that involvement was either improving or already high.Improvements to involvement in the project included additional advertisements and further promotion through community forums and events. One respondent mentioned the need to address a perception that World Vision and its partners earned money through the project: ‘people think we earn money so people have different mentalities’. However, among respondents there was a sense that World Vision is a strongly trusted partner in the community:

World Vision is there for everybody; look after the people; making trainings etc.

This was clearly enhanced by the physical presence of World Vision in the area; two respondents referred to the visibility of the World Vision office and staff.

Economic Development Focus

We also asked twenty-two additional open-ended questions, relating specifically to economic development. These were largely directed towards community members (as well as one of the partners, the SMME Forum). These were broadly categories under two headings:

  1. Perceived current situation and options
  2. Perceptions of / preferred options for future improvement

Current Situation and Options

We asked a total of 8 questions concerning the current economic situation and options in Orlando East. Due to overlapping content and time constraints, not all questions were asked of all respondents – we obtained between 3 (question 8a) and 12 responses.

Current options

In response to question ED1, ‘What are the currently available options for jobs or earning income?’, responses outlined a range of perceived options, including:

  • Recycling
  • Installing and maintaining energy and water facilities
  • Bakeries
  • Sports coaching
  • Recycling & buy-back centres
  • Handiworks
  • Car washes
  • Selling fast food (‘bunny chows’)
  • Performing and teaching music
  • PC sales and maintenance
  • Hospitality: restaurants, B&B’s and kiosks
  • Construction

Curiously, respondents didn’t emphasise some of the options which they themselves were employed in, such as hairdressing or administration. This suggests that even current employment options were not necessarily synonymous with jobs held by respondents.

Respondents also referred to characteristics they thought important for finding jobs or earning income. Similar to community responses above, factors such as skills, education and self-reliance or independence featured in multiple responses. Two small business owners also expressed doubt about the utility of available finance options for expanding their business. One respondent, a PC reseller, stated: “I believe I should save money for myself rather than taking loans because at the end it is difficult to repay them”.

The SMME Forum, which largely represents skilled tradespeople, mentioned that they expected prospects in the construction industry to improve in 2013 due to government funding of 15 ‘superblocks’ in the area. This would increase the availability of sub-contracting work.

Information about Jobs and Economic Opportunities

In response to questions ED2 (‘How would you find out about employment and access better economic opportunities?’) and ED3 (‘What are the sources of information on employment, business opportunities, skills training etc.?’), responses identified a number of common sources of information. These include:

  • Press reports
  • Billboards
  • Flyers and posters
  • Radio
  • Urban newspapers
  • Government service providers
  • A local skills centre (since closed down)
  • Libraries
  • World Vision
  • Websites

Again the SMME Forum provided considerable detail on the process of locating employment and business opportunity. Based on interview data, they believe 70-80% of people they interview ‘have the potential to run their own business’. The Forum understands its role to provide a level of practical skill and business management to would-be entrepreneurs. In their view, there is a very strong need for small businesses in the area to acquire business and financial management skills, and they welcomed World Vision’s support in running financial training sessions recently. Towards that end, they are currently developing a member database to match CVs and skills with available opportunities, and have also petitioned government to reduce the overhead to establish a business. Practically, they offer common sense advice to young people considering starting their own business. A key question they often ask is:

What do they really want to do? What kind of business, and why?

need to ask foreigners how best to run their business rather than complaining.

Options for Future Improvement

General Feelings about the Future

Question ED9 asked ‘Do you feel things are looking better when you look into the future? Why/why not?’. Consistent with the general optimism expressed regarding prospects (ED4), respondents were confident that ‘things will pick up’. A lack of skills is a major obstacle facing small businesses. Some people with technical skills lack business skills and some business people do not have adequate technical skills.   2. Lack of preparedness - e.g. what is the gap in the market?

People are unemployed; when community leaders are employed, they become rich for families, not for the whole community; they sell some work, a form of bribery, only helping their friends to go up (particularly in government)… bribery is “ripe”

Removing Obstacles and Creating Opportunities

Questions ED12a and ED13a were again closely related, examining ways obstacles could be removed and opportunities created. ED12a asked ‘Why do you believe your community is in a position to change or influence these obstacles?’ and ED13a asked ‘Why do you believe your community is in a position to create opportunities?’. Responses here were somewhat vague and hence difficult to summarise. In contrast to the preceding answers, a number of respondents replied that the community needed both attitudinal and behavioural change. For example, the manager of Footprints replied ‘People can only smile when they get up in the morning’. One respondent thought that the community needed ‘to give him a chance to show his work’, while another stated:

Motivation must be provided to people to encourage them and show appreciation of what they are doing…. People don’t appreciate a good thing until something wrong happens.

The manager of the SMME forum suggested focussing on additional avenues to government for opportunities: ‘Businesses have social responsibility; people are not tapping into that’. A further limitation, in his view, was that while the potential existed to create new opportunities, ‘there was a tendency for discussions not to reach the implementation stage’. Nevertheless, there had been some positive developments and ‘success has encouraged people’.

A common material requirement, particularly among those working in the informal and community development sector, was for better basic equipment for gardening, hairdressing and other small business opportunities. One respondent pointed to the ability of government to create opportunities through investment in ‘infrastructure, music festivals, new stadium’.

Livelihoods in the City

Question ED14 focussed on the relationship between economic development and the urban environment, asking ‘Could you describe ways in which being in a city makes it easier to earn your livelihood? How does the city make it harder to earn your livelihood?’. Responses were roughly split. Several respondents mentioned the greater availability of work:

In Johannesburg, easier to get “petty money” compared with rural areas.

You can make a living if you put your pride aside.

Urban opportunities are much better than opportunities in rural areas, although city by-laws are restrictive and can make life difficult.

Two respondents mentioned downsides of the urban environment: high crime rates and the fact that it was a ‘dog eat dog’ world.

Future Options

Questions ED15 and ED16 focussed on future options for economic development. Both asked variants of ‘What do people see as their best options for economic development?’. Most responses focussed on small business opportunities, including:

  • Waste management
  • Food produce and urban agriculture
  • Bricklaying, painting, paving
  • IT
  • Tourism
  • Working in their own businesses
  • House renovations
  • Car washing
  • Health
  • Sport (and associated industries)

The SMME Forum again provided a useful overview of potential areas of economic growth:

People’s own businesses provide the best economic opportunities. Local businesses keep more people and money in Soweto. Lack of local shopping complexes. Both grass roots expansion and expansion via partnerships with bigger organisations (e.g. the Master Builders’ Federation) are needed. Bigger organisations have the advantage of providing more training. It’s possible for large multinationals and local home-grown businesses to work together. Established companies - mainly white - can help local businesses get BEE compliance; improve scorecard ratings, improve skills transfer.

Question ED17 asked a follow-up question relating to the degree of control respondents felt they had over such options (‘In your view, do you think you are able to influence the economic options available to you? Or is the context too dynamic and changing too rapidly?’). This was asked of three respondents. One respondent was positive about the potential for small business ‘to undercut shops in the shopping malls’, and viewed advertising as particularly important to driving business growth. Another felt ‘adapting to change is difficult because the pace of change is so rapid’.

Examining the Bigger Picture

Questions ED18, ED19 and ED20 examined broader and longer term trends affecting economic options. Question ED18 asked “Do you see yourself as still working in this type of work in five years’ time? If not, why not?” Three out of four respondents weren’t certain; one replied that they “Don’t want to stay in the same work but would not give up on it”. One respondent, working at the community Environmental Forum, was optimistic that this was a growth area, since ‘our levels of understanding on environmental issues is increasing’.

Question ED19 asked ‘What changes to this sector would you like to see?’. Two respondents reaffirmed the need for ‘modernised equipment’ and infrastructure, while a third mentioned he would like to see ‘changes in local regulations to make them less restrictive’.

Question ED20 asked ‘Do you expect national or international economic trends to limit your ability to raise income in the future?’. Three out of four respondents weren’t sure or didn’t think foreign events had an undue impact on them in the foreseeable future, other than to increase prices. The manager of Footprints felt that foreign ‘investors’, including NGOs, were playing an important role in providing funds for ‘sports, education, health’.

Orlando East

Question ED21 examined the specific context of Orlando East, asking ‘What aspects of Orlando East do you feel attract people? What aspects of Orlando East either deters people from coming here or encourages them to leave?’. We received ten responses to this question. Two themes stood out: the vital role of Orlando East in the political history of South Africa, and the recently built Orlando Stadium, which hosted games for the 2010 World Cup.

In relation to its historical role, several respondents pointed to its importance as a drawcard for tourism. The SMME forum manager replied:

Orlando East has the unique feature of its historical role in the apartheid era. This has provided the basis for tourism, OE’s biggest industry. Tourism could be expanded much more.

Another respondent also pointed to the importance of key events in the area:

Politics is #1. Orlando Communal Hall is where the break between the ANC and Pan-Africa Congress parties happened – a major political event in the 1950s. 1960s race riots – apartheid is the “history of Orlando East”.

Closely related to the area’s history is the prominence of sport – highlighted by the Orlando Stadium, the largest and most clearly visible building in the area. As one respondents put it:

Things that attract people are the soccer stadium. E.g. Williams sisters were here. Used to have a tennis court – caretaker did not allow practices there. Swimming pool. Could be a diversity of sports interests.

Other respondents gave mixed responses. Some pointed to the lack of change and closure of facilities motivating people to leave, to ‘go to greener pastures’. On the other hand, Orlando East continued to attract migrants from the rest of Africa and parts of South Africa. Several respondents referenced the quality of ‘Ubuntu’, a concept that references the importance of community and ‘love for one another’, as a feature of Soweto that brings people back to visit or stay in the community.

A Modest Investment…

Question ED22 asked a hypothetical question about investment: ‘If you had R 1000 to invest, how would you use this to maximise the benefit for you and your family? Has the World Vision project changed how you would use this money now compared to 3 years ago?’

The small business operators we asked suggested they would invest the money in their business, buying new equipment or experimenting with new products. One respondent suggested he would ‘put 500 in bank; [and use the] other 500 to improve business’. The SMME Forum manager used the question to describe the difficulties many without financial literacy experience when dealing with money:

How a windfall of R1000 would be used would depend on who received it. ‘Rich people buy assets, poor people buy things.’. There is a great lack of awareness regarding financial management, banking and debt. There is a need to increase financial literacy. People get credit - then debt. Don’t understand bank accounts [or the] value of owning property over debt. Education needs to be early.

Partner Results

Partnering is considered a foundational part of the Orlando East project, and of World Vision’s Urban Programming generally. Accordingly, although project partners in this instance had much to say of interest about the Orlando East community and its challenges, we restrict discussion of the partner interviews to questions to those that relate to partnering and networking. As we discuss below, these do however relate closely to broader issues of the Sowetan urban environment.

A total of 21 qualitative or open-ended questions were constructed around the partnership theme. As with the community and staff surveys, not all questions were deemed relevant in all instances, and we noted considerable overlap in any case between similarly worded questions. Because of this repetition, we have consolidated responses around a series of more general partner themes:

  • General nature and assessment of the partnership (questions 31-33, 44, 47)
  • Financial arrangements (questions 34-36)
  • Procedural arrangements (questions 37-40, 49)
  • World Vision’s role (questions 45-46; 48; 50-51)
  • Recommentations (questions 52a)

The findings are discussed below against these themes.

General nature and assessment of the partnership

Partner respondents were positive about the collaborative and equity aspects of their relationship with World Vision. One organisation, Dependable Strengths, felt its recent relationship with World Vision had been mutually beneficial:

Yes. Partnership has furthered both partner goals. [The partner had observed that:] 1. WV connects young people to other services 2. People can go to the next level in the job market

Similarly, an HIV/AIDS care facility operated by a community-based organisation called Footprints felt that World Vision was an essential ‘pillar in the community’, both providing essential items like medicine trolleys and wheelchairs, and acting as a broker to connect the organisation with other partners.

Two partners expressed the concern that while their goals were shared, it was difficult to realise those goals due to resource constraints. One partner, the local Fire Department, suggested ‘would be preferable if WV covered a larger physical area’.

Overall, though, respondents were confident that their current partnering arrangements with World Vision shared common goals. They also agreed these arrangements were largely on a equitable basis.

There was some difference in interpretation around this question through; a number of respondents considered ‘equity’ in the context of broader community and partner network context. In some cases, this brought out points of criticism. For example, the manager of the Victim Empowerment Unit complained specifically about the lack of support offered by the South African Police Service (SAPS). In the context of the work her unit is responsible for, ‘police support [is] critical to provide security’. Two other partners, both affiliated with community organisations, suggested there was a problem with gender equity and representation. This suggests that World Vision has an ongoing role in advocating and addressing equity issues within the broader partnering context.

In response to question 33, ‘what are the most important elements of a good partnership?’, respondents raised a number of themes: common goals; open communication and transparency; mutual recognition and support; acknowledgement of how all partners benefit; and, in the words of one respondent, ‘allowing partners to grow both horizontally and vertically’. Question 44 elicited similar responses; here all respondents mentioned the importance of having a ‘clarification of roles’ and ‘a common vision’. One respondent also spoke of the importance of having this backed up by ‘low level’ activities, such as attending eachother’s events and talking about ‘achievements and challenges’.

Financial arrangements

Responses to the questions about funding and finances were quite limited. In most cases, the partnerships appeared to be largely non-financial; World Vision contributed mostly in-kind support, with occasional funding for particular workshops or events. Partners were generally concerned about the lack of resources; the Early Childhood Development group noted one example of the difficulties of multilateral funding arrangements, when ‘a building material supply company withdrew its offer of building materials when it realised the amount of materials required for a particular schools project’. None of the partners saw World Vision’s role as a funding partner.

Procedural arrangements

Questions 37-40 and 49 examined the process by which the partnership(s) had been formed. Question 37 asked whether roles are clearly defined. All respondents were agreed emphatically that this was the case. One respondent claimed that ‘World Vision delivered on their promises’, while another stated:

The roles of World Vision and the Fire Department are clearly defined and understood.

Question 38 asked whether project planning processes were collaborative. Again respondents were unanimously in agreement. As one respondent put it:

Yes; everybody from the top down has been part of the process. Things are discussed, sifted.

In their response to this question, Footprints, an organisation that offered hospital beds and treatment to HIV/AIDS sufferers, outlined a process where they shared an annual Strategic Plan with partners. This seemed to facilitate greater awareness of their mission, and foster stronger collaboration with partners.

Question 39 asked whether, and how often, meetings were held between partners to discuss issues and challenges. This elicited a range of responses, from ‘there are frequent meetings held between the two partners’ to ‘meetings between the partners are not frequent’. In the remaining cases, it appeared that there had been a number of initial face-to-face meetings, followed by sporadic or monthly phone calls. One respondent stated the World Vision team was ‘just a phone call away’; another suggested ‘they will come to us, when they need us’.

Question 40 asked whether Monitoring & Evaluation (M&E) stategies had been agreed upon between partners. Again this elicited a range of responses, from ‘there are no formal monitoring and evaluation procedures in place yet’ to ‘yes we do agree on monitoring a specific project that we will be doing’. In most cases, it appeared that project reporting activities are extensions of existing organisational reporting activities, and therefore reflected extrinsic characteristics such as organisational maturity, resources and priorities. In several cases, partners expressed a desire to document particular models or processess, in order that their work be recognised and replicated. This was evocatively captured in the description given by the Program Manager of PlanAct:

This model needs to be documented and shared, to replicate. Have been approached to facilitate in other areas (of Joburg). Beginning to see ‘spin offs’ in the local area. Using local newspaper to get the message out.

Question 49 asked respondents how formal they considered the partnership to be. Two out of four responses stated the relationship was informal, as nothing had been provided in writing. In other two cases, some kind of written agreement or MOU existed. Anecdotal feedback from other partners suggested that there was a roughly even split between formal and informal arrangements in place.

World Vision’s role

Overall, although these responses were aimed at understanding the nature of the partner network generally, they strongly suggest that World Vision is held in very high regard, as a partner that is reliable, organised, inclusive, consultative and available. Questions 45, 46, 48, 50 and 51 broadly examine World Vision’s role in the project and associated partnerships, with a particular focus on the future sustainability of projects and partnerships.

Question 48 asked respondents to describe World Vision’s role in relation to the project and, more broadly, to the local community. All six respondents to this question used either the words ‘facilitator’ or ‘catalyst’. This was linked to other roles World Vision was assumed to play: as a keeper of ‘institutional memories’; as a trusted source in the community; as an organiser of opportunities and operator of programs.

Question 45 asked how World Vision had specifically facilitated the development of partnerships, and capacity building of the partner network in the area. Respondents described a very broad range of roles and actions World Vision had undertaken. These include both direct economic development activities – helping to create businesses, and developing financial and practical skills, particularly among young people – as well as auxiliary work in assisting creches and churches, and advocating for specific issues such as child abuse.

Question 50 asked whether the ‘community network’ existed before World Vision’s involvement. This question did not prove to be especially relevant, as in most cases World Vision had been instrumental in creating a ‘community network’ through the partnership itself. The Fire Department did reply:

Previous to working with WV, the Fire Department worked with local councillors. They also partner with Nelson Mandela Children Fund to raise awareness on the dangers of fire.

Questions 46 and 51 both relate to the future sustainability of work done through the partnership arrangement. Question 46 asked how World Vision is preparing network partners for for future financial autonomy. All partners agreed that World Vision had been actively promoting autonomy; as one respondent put it, ‘they implement something and give it to the community to carry on’. Another partner appeared concerned that since their project involved ‘working with people with no money’, they found it ‘very difficult’ and were ‘hoping for finance, to spend money on training’. In general, the more established the partner, the more capable they seemed of continuing their work without World Vision’s support. Footprints stated that ‘World Vision has advised several times on the need for self-sufficiency’ – and elaborated on a series of measures they had adopted in current and subsequent plans to increase their financial independence. Such measures included training other organisations; delivering services; andsupplying commercial companies with gardening produce such as peas and carrots.

Question 51 asked, more directly, ‘If WV was removed today from the network, would it be self-sustaining? If so, why do you think this is? If not, why not?’ This brought a more diverse range of responses. One partner thought World Vision had already helped people ‘to fend for themselves’, and that community could ‘not expect people to be given things’. Another felt this would ‘be fair for the communities’, but at the same time expressed the view that ‘if World Vision is removed we will be blind because World Vision is our eyes in the community’. Two other respondents stated that their projects were not yet self-sustaining. As the manager of Footprints put it:

Have not reached that stage yet. This is a process of working with people. Invest five years of time in this project.

Our limited observations during the week concurred with this view. As we discuss in the analysis below, World Vision continues to play a foundational and formative role in the community projects and partnerships it has initiated.

Recommentations and Achievements

Questions 52a, 100a and 101 asked respondents to propose recommendations, to reflect on past achievements and to state their future goals for the partnership and project.

Recommendations

Questions 52a asked specifically ‘do you have any recommendations about ways World Vision could improve how it works with partners in this project?’ The main response was to expand the operational scale and geographical reach of World Vision in the area. One respondent felt that through the partnership, more could be done for ‘orphans and vulnerable children and to [combat] child abuse’. Another suggested relocating the World Vision office more centrally, as ‘there is a challenge of going up there’.

Past Achievements

In response to question 100a, concerning past achievements, all respondents were able to outline particular goals that had been met. The Womens Forum, a small co-operative, emphasised ‘providing training and skills to local people’. Thlokomelo, a child protection organisation operating out of the same premises as World Vision, outlined its growth from being ‘based in a shack’, without a ‘kitchen with running water’, to an important provider of food and social work services for children. Footprints stated its most signicant achievement had been the opening of the hospice. Dependable Strengths, a training organisation based in downtown Johannesburg, had conducted three training sessions with the support of World Vision, and found, after surveying 37 attendees:

  • 13 had gained employment
  • 7 were studying further
  • 4 initiated businesses (in tourism, hairdressing)
  • 9 were involved in community work (stipendry), with organisations such as the YMCA
  • only 4 had had no progress

Finally, other partners emphasised less tangible but not less critical achievements: raising awareness and advocating on behalf of child abuse victims, and building relationships and improving communication with the community.

Future Goals

Question 101 asked ‘Thinking ahead, what single achievement would you most like to see happen in the next 3 years?’. This question was asked of all partners, and elicited a wide range of responses – some focussed on community impact, others addressing aspects of the partnership. As these responses shed light on the broader challenges of the community, they are quoted or paraphrased at length.

The Womens Forum stated they would like their work to be recognised throughout South Africa. To achieve that, they are looking to market themselves, as well as to partner with others. To do so, they need greater specificity of skills and training.

Thlokomelo stated that their vision is focussed on children and young people. In their words:

Would like to see them start businesses, to improve themselves and the community. Do 3 years of university. SA short of engineers; would like to see area produce an engineer, doctor, maybe even the PM. Professionals work from the ground to the top.

The Victim Empowerment Unit saw the most important goal as being the alleviation of poverty through job creation. The manager stated she ‘would like to see people having long-term, sustainable jobs and healthy lifestyles’. To do so, they ‘need to be educated - Educate, educate, educate’. However this goal was currently being compromised by unstable project resourcing and corruption.

Dependable Strengths articulated tremendous confidence in thier specific training program. Participants experienced ‘a transformation in the room’, and got ‘hooked on the program’. The managers of the business expressed a desire to ‘change South Africa one person at a time’; they were looking to scale up the program and ‘roll [it] out throughout the country’. They hope to continue the relationship with World Vision, acknowledging that this ‘all depends on finance’.

Footprints stated that their most pressing goal was ‘to be able to sustain ourselves’. They felt World Vision was providing constructive advice for ‘working towards that’.

Pikitup, a waste management company, aimed to increase education about waste and the environment, particularly among children. In their words, ‘a cleever child will educate the parent’. To do so, they want to build a team of professionals in the environmental field. They also hope to expand the relationship with World Vision over the next three years.

PlanAct expressed a desire to ‘create opportunities for people to have their livelihoods secured’. They hoped to commence a new project with World Vision to help facilitate this.

In spite of the diversity of goals, all partners hoped to continue to expand their relationship with World Vision in the future.

Staff Results

Project Design, Monitoring and Evaluation (DM&E)

Staff had several reflections on DM&E experiences in the Orlando East project with implications for urban programming.

During the assessment and design phase, it was suggested than an ‘environmental scan’ be carried out to ensure that new projects do not duplicate what others are already doing. The pre-existing activities of other local actors should be mapped against the objectives set for any new project. A ‘group-based’ targeting approach was also suggested, rather than conventional methods of working at an individual or household level. It was considered key to be working with networks and ‘big forums’ in order to have a stronger shared voice in negotiating with the City of Johannesburg government. To this end, the Orlando East project has tried to ‘use different approaches for different communities’, and prioritise the most vulnerable organisations over the more developed ones. There is a greater emphasis on ‘small’ partners whom the project ‘treats as equals.’

According to staff, project timeframes need to be sensitive to stakeholders in the urban context. There was a sense of a demonstrative element required in World Vision work, such that:

People want to see something come up first before they want to be part of what we are doing.

This reflection would appear to support the concept of a single-issue urban entry project that builds trust and relationships with local stakeholders in a shorter span of time.25

In the case of Orlando East, a period of six months was required simply to ‘adapt and understand the context.’ The following two years were required to ‘get the project running’. One staff member recommended a greater frequency of redesign events (annual), to cater for changes in the urban environment such as movement of local people.

Learning and reflection activities involving staff, and also community members and partners, were considered a fundamental aspect of urban program implementation. One staff member described it as such:

When you are involved in the game, you cannot be the referee. You need someone to stop the game and talk about the rules.

Staff members appreciated opportunities to reflect on their experiences, and were particularly concerned with the documentation of lessons. Monthly monitoring and semi-annual reporting (as part of the LEAP cycle) was considered an opportunity to reflect on lessons – with community members as well as staff.

Finally, the accountability element of DM&E was seen as valuable to keep projects focused, however LEAP was seen as a toolkit that should be ‘adaptive and flexible’ when needed. One suggestion was that the logframe as a tool is ‘not suitable’ for urban contexts, and was considered ‘too rigid with too many assumptions’ being made about urban issues.

Child Well-being in urban contexts

Staff considered the project to be making ‘indirect’ contributions to child well-being, with a stated frustration that, to achieve the outcome chosen from the Compendium (‘Children educated for life’), ‘there are many things that you have to get through in between.’ ‘Friendly spaces for children to learn, such as early childhood development’(ECD) centres, were suggested as an important contribution that urban ’projects could make to child well-being.

One staff member also suggested that a single issue focus could actually have a deeper impact on overall child well-being – ‘doing one thing and doing it good’. This reflects the fact that the Community Development Committee (CDC) – initially approached to work on economic development – has diversified into sub-groups that are also addressing issues of child protection, education, health and positive relationships in the community.

It was agreed among staff interview respondents that child sponsorship was not suitable for application in urban contexts, predominantly due to mobility in the target community. It should be noted, however, that more detailed data on the specific challenges of child sponsorship in urban was not gathered during these interviews.26

Partnering

‘Development as partnership’ is a theme that consistently emerged from the staff interviews. The approach of the Orlando East project is not considered to be a collection of direct interventions, but rather a case of ‘working through partners’ wherever possible. A practical example of financial partnering between organisations in Orlando East was given as equitable sharing of payments for activities or services - ‘50/50 with big organisations’. In these arrangmements, no cash changes hands between partner organisations.

During the interviews with staff there was a repeated emphasis on the intangible nature of urban projects, such that:

People want to come and see – but sorry we don’t have a vegetable garden. If you don’t have those things, [the question is asked] what are you doing? This is the whole issue of facilitation.

It became clear that throughout the course of these interviews that although urban facilitation and partnering were well understood at the field or project- level, there is a need for a better understanding of the implications within World Vision at other levels. This is also reflected in the results from questions on organisational systems (see below).

Role of World Vision

The role of World Vision in Orlando East is variously described as ‘working through partners,’ ‘coordination,’ ‘connecting and facilitating,’ ‘education’ and ‘advocacy’. All of these descriptors would seem to be valid, as the World Vision project staff play a variety of roles:

  • linking and referring community members to available services
  • raising the profile of community-based organisations and welfare groups
  • providing a shared platform for disparate groups to meet and work together on common objectives
  • improving the flow of information between the various individuals and groups present in Orlando East.

On a daily basis, staff activities were described as consisting of:

  • hosting and running meetings for the purposes of planning, or reporting on progress
  • participating in public events or gatherings
  • conducting clear and transparent communication with project stakeholders (telephone or email)
  • providing advice or responding to enquiries from local residents, which might result in referrals to service providers, or contacting local authorities e.g. police

At face value these activities might seem somewhat banal but in fact are conducted with a high degree of sensitivity to stakeholder interests and requirements, political undertones, and underlying ethical considerations such as equity and the inclusion of marginalised groups.

Trust, transparency and ‘openness’ were operating principles also repeated by several staff members:

A lot of maturity is needed to do this work… [and] you can’t afford to stop communicating even on the little things.

In addition, a core staff competency is the ability to ‘understand urban politics’, especially the ways in which government officials carry out their work. Staff considered the act of linking local residents to their government representatives to be a value-adding role of the project. Community members were seen to be generally ‘pulling back’ from government processes, unless there is a perceived gain or benefit to be made. Volunteerism in the community is also low.

Before this project there was no platform to engage in a healthy and active way; in a way that appreciates constraints and challenges on both sides. The project helps them knock on right doors and be in contact with the right people.

By extension, holding government to account for its responsibilities was also seen as critical for the Orlando East project:

Our role is to ensure that government is in the picture, delivering services to the people, doing what they are supposed to be doing.

Local Economic Development

The context of Orlando East and Soweto was seen by staff to be more conducive to economic development programming, due to the high turnover of cash, services and goods in the urban economy.

Sustainability

The project’s efforts were considered to be having a positive impact on institutions that will exist after World Vision leaves the area. Working through networks and ‘big forums’ was also seen to be a key factor in strengthening sustainability of the project.

According to staff, one of the most important objectives the project could have in the near-term is training and building the capacity of partners to ensure sustainability. For example, the ability to ‘resource funding’ through proposal- writing was raised as an important local skill, coupled with the need to:

Inform partners that we are not here as donors, we are here as partners.

Organisational systems and internal processes

According to respondents, facilitation as an activity does not translate well into World Vision systems, especially finance. Finance process requirements are considered by staff to be overly bureaucratic, and inflexible to the demands of the project context. A repeated example, small but not insignificant, was the provision of refreshments during meetings with partners. The approval process for this expense was considered too long and cumbersome, despite how frequently it is required.

Your bill for the month is food and petrol – I am asked ‘what is it that you are doing?’ When you are building capacity, or going to meetings, that is your expense. In urban space you don’t focus on the physical.

In addition, internal finance policies were seen to be constantly changing and poorly communicated, adding to the difficulties in managing urban project budgets effectively.

This is particularly relevant, as discussed below under Lessons Learnt, to establishing relationships between dependent and independent variables in projects’ Theories of Change.

Partner Results

The Partner Analysis Tool is a generic instrument used by VicHealth and was trialled as part of the Partner Survey. We administered the tool to five out of eight partners (in the remaining three cases we either ran out of time or did not consider the tool appropriate to use in the context). The tool includes 35 Likert items, scaled from 1 (“Strongly disagree”) to 5 (“Strongly agree”), grouped in the following seven stages of partnership development:

  1. Determining the need for the partnership
  2. Choosing partners
  3. Making sure partnerships work
  4. Planning collaborative action
  5. Implementing collaborative action
  6. Minimising the barriers to partnerships
  7. Reflecting on and continuing the partnership

The tool suggests three “bands” for interpreting the aggregate scores of the tool:

35-84 The whole idea of a partnership should be rigorously questioned.

85-126 The partnership is moving in the right direction but it will need more attention if it is going to be really successful.

127-175 A partnership based on genuine collaboration has been established. The challenge is to maintain its impetus and build on the current success.

The average aggregate scores for the five participants was 139, comfortably in the top band suggesting that a “partnership based on genuine collaboration has been established”. Only one respondent rated the partnership in the middle band of “it will need more attention if it is going to be really successful”. In this case additional qualitative evidence made it clear this was because the “partnership” in this case referred to a multilateral rather than bilateral relationship, and the respondent was quite critical of work done by one of the other partners (the SA police). This reflects a difficulty concerning whether the underlying construct being measured by World Vision under “partnering” relates to a specific one-to-one relationship between World Vision and a given interviewee, or to a broader notion of “partner network”. Several draft survey questions used the term “network”; however in piloting we discarded the term, since most of the relationships were in fact bilateral–meaning that World Vision seems to function, in this project, as the centre hub around which other organisations form relationships, rather than just one node or point on a network.27 While the network idea is attractive–particularly from the point of view of establishing sustainability beyond World Vision’s own involvement in the project–in this project it is yet to be realised. One possibility, stemming from these observations, is to develop a specific indicator to measure the maturity of the network itself. In cases, like Orlando East where World Vision is for the most part the lead partner in a two-way partnership arrangement, this may suggest a relative lack of maturity. Improving upon this maturity would be a worth side aim or goal of building capacity at both community and partner levels.

Analysis

Overview

The findings offer a rich set of insights into the general and economic challenges faced by the Orlando East community. As the first time that World Vision’s Urban Meta-analysis Framework (UMF) has been trialled as a conceptual tool for developing questions and indicators, they also provide valuable insights into future directions of urban programming reviews and evaluations. In this section we summarise key findings and explore implications for both the Orlando East project and other urban projects.

Orlando East Project Findings

Discussion of Community Findings

The community findings show that, on the whole, and in spite of considerable signs of resilience and optimism in the community, life in Orlando East in 2012 remains difficult. The responses to questions about situational assessment, vulnerability and inequality drew out a range of specific concerns, including the prevalence of HIV/AIDS, drugs, crime, lawlessness and poverty. While no surprise to development practitioners in the area, this contrasts with the generally positive image conveyed of post-apartheid South Africa internationally.

Such difficulties are further reflected in the generally despondent views towards both government and the Orlando East community. In particular respondents indicated a high level of concern with government inaccessability, lack of accountability, apathy and corruption. Although we had meetings scheduled with several council members from the local government, these were unfortunately cancelled – in hindsight the review would have benefited from responses from local government officials with direct accountability to the area. In spite of this, it is clear from other responses (as well as direct observation in the area) that government has a crucial and ongoing role in economic development in Orlando East. The prominence of ‘government’ in response to questions that didn’t directly ask about governance issues (especially in relation to the economic development questions) attests to this.

In passing, we note the very specific role of Orlando East in the development of the ANC during the apartheid regime adds a particular poignancy to respondents’ observations concerning the lack of government support today. Numerous respondents talked with considerable pride about the recent history of the area, and indeed, ‘historic’ tourism is a noted form of economic growth. Despite many obvious signs of recent physical infrastructural improvements – not only the most prominent example of the Orlando East stadium, but also general upgrades to housing and roads – responses demonstrated a concern about the absence of less perspicuous, ‘soft’ urban infrastructure, particularly in the areas of education, health and transport. We note also that the current government has had less than 20 years in which to reverse the effects of systemic ‘undevelopment’ in the area, during which time both Orlando and Soweto generally has experienced significant population growth28. Nonetheless, both the participants’ responses and background data suggests there remains much that government actors, particularly, need to do to advance economic growth.

World Vision itself seemed to be held in very high regard by those community members we interviewed – findings backed up by both the qualitative and quantitative results. Mean scores for questions about World Vision scored highly (7.33), while question relating to citizen rights and government accountability scored poorly (3.63; 3.80), relative both to other items and an expected mean of 5.5. From the qualitative feedback, World Vision were seen to be particularly helpful in relation to the issue of poverty. From the perspective of urban development, respondents who were aware of World Vision’s economic development project also had warmed to the different role it had played since closing down as an ADP and focussing on local economic development. Both World Vision staff were also highly respected. On the one hand, this is clearly pleasing from an organisational point of view; on the other, it poses a distinct problem in terms of the sustainability of the project beyond World Vision’s direct involvement and funding. We discuss this issue further in relation to the idea of a ‘partnership network’ in the Partner section below. We also note here that we see a very specific role World Vision can continue to play, given the high (and in the context, rare) level of trust it holds, in advocating on behalf of the community to government (at all levels).

Discussion of Partner Findings

Partner responses to qualitative questions show very strong support for their relationships with World Vision. This was reinforced by the five sets of responses to the Partnership Assessment Tool, where the average overall score was in the highest band of responses. Partners generally praised World Vision’s ability to operate equitable, well organised and mutually beneficial partnerships. This is testament not only to the general regard for World Vision, but also to the trust and respect engendered by the current team and project. Admittedly we only spoke to partners organised by the World Vision team itself, so results may be affected by a skewed or biased sample. Moreover, as we discuss below, a number of partners in this context are highly dependent on any support they can find, and are likely to be highly complementary of those that provide it. Nevertheless, World Vision team members were frequently not present during interviews, and responses seemed genuine rather than manufactured.

The success cultivation of partnerships since the projects’ inception does lead to a curious and unintended problem of dependency. In the Urban Meta-analysis Framework we developed to guide the development of questions, a key concept is that of a “partner network”. In most cases in Orlando East, it appeared that partnerships were bilateral – between World Vision and another organisation – rather than multilateral. There were certainly cases where the ‘partner’ itself was a conglomerate of individuals, businesses and other organisations – this was the case for the various community forum representatives we interviewed for example. However, responses to questions 46 and 51 suggest that most of the partnerships are reliant upon World Vision as a facilitator, and indeed could not be sustained without its involvement. Interestingly, this is not because of direct funding. None of the partners had received financial support from World Vision. Rather it appears that the tremendous social and human capital an international NGO of World Vision’s size brings to the table – as well as its history in the area – is of immense importance to partners. Even more established organisations, such as Footprints and the SMME Forum, referenced occasions when World Vision had put them in touch with other organisations to deliver financial and training support.

One way of imagining this role is through a network map of relationships. In 2009, the authors of the Baseline Report (World Vision South Africa 2010a) conducted a network mapping exercise with the local Community Development Committee. This showed Government at the centre of the diagram (though the authors noted World Vision and PlanAct ‘currently mediated’ relationships between the Committee and government authorities). Though we did not conduct such an exercise as part of this review, we suspect a contemporary version would show World Vision being in the centre of such a diagram – at least for the partners we interviewed. Just as critically, World Vision appeared to also be an important facilitator of relationships between its partners, and the broader community and government. This may also reflect the changed role of WV since the inception of a single issue economic development project. Although not necessarily a desirable outcome (i.e. WV being at the ‘centre’ of a network) this might reflect a certain stage of project maturity, where in future WV could transition into a network ‘node’ rather than anchor.

The idea that World Vision in practice occupies something of the facilitation role that local government should play in theory is not, however, particularly far fetched, as the community responses show. It does raise several pertinent questions, both in the Orlando East context and for urban programming in general:

  1. How can World Vision transition a series of bilateral relationships into a multilateral network by the completion of a project?
  2. Given the human resource intensivity of partnership and advocacy work, how can this kind of programming model scale up (both within a project, and to other projects in related areas and cities)?
  3. Is ‘project’ even a fitting description of the kind of engagement World Vision is making here?
  4. And to what extent, then, are conventional project management tools such as the logframe and financial instruments appropriate for the conduct of such an engagement?

As we note in the analysis of staff findings, there has been some friction between the organisational and reporting requirements and the day-to-day practice of operating the project. We suspect this relates to the same kinds of issues, which we endeavour to dissect in greater detail in the discussion of urban programming and the meta-analysis framework below.

Discussion of Staff Findings

In general, staff findings confirm observations from the community and partner interviews. Staff were similarly enthusiastic about the transition from child sponsorship to a focus on the single issue of economic development (“doing one thing and doing it good”), even if the links between the two seem at times tenuous (“there are many things have to get through in between”). All staff were able to point to specific achievements over the past three years that had contributed to both economic development and child well-being.

However their deeper and more intimate knowledge of both the context and the project allowed for considerable reflective criticism of how World Vision engages with the urban environment. Two primary – and related – frustrations emerged in this respect:

  1. Staff had difficulty showing evidence of progress due to the intangible, “soft” nature of their work (“sorry we don’t have a vegetable garden”).
  2. Staff also found it cumbersome requesting funding for the sorts of activities that accompany partnering, facilitation and advocacy (“In the urban space you don’t focus on the physical”).

As we touch on further below at a more abstract level, these frustrations relate strongly to the character of development work in cities. At a basic level, the Orlando East team of 2 are operating in a relatively small spatial zone comprising between 4 and 6 million people (in the greater Soweto). It was clear from all of the responses that the strategy of providing “soft” infrastructure – facilitation, partnering skills, advocacy – was the most practical way to effect transformation, both qualitatively and quantitatively, in the most vulnerable in the community. Relative to “hard” infrastracture (such as the cited “well” example), these activities are difficult both to measure and justify. Indeed, it is likely to be the case that indicators of project impact in these cases will be highly qualitative, difficult to measure and subject to varying interpretations – a point, again, we return to in the discussion of evaluation methodology below. It appears, in particular, that project staff would operate more effectively with a more flexible accounting regime, so long as the funded activities can be reconciled to project outcomes.

Staff were less concerned about the actual DM&E mechanisms (though logframe was criticised in this respect) for reporting on urban programming. Indeed responses showed an appreciation of the “forced” reflection these mechanisms encouraged. The lengthy initial “environmental scan” proved to be a useful way to understand the focus of the project, and how best World Vision could contribute. Given the frequent change in the environment, more frequent though smaller-scale scans and reviews would also be helpful, to hone and adjust the focus of the project over time (a process of such regular reviewing appeared to happen informally in any case). The staging of the World Cup in 2010 proved to be an important event of the sort that could also trigger adjustment of project methods.

Despite the positive signs shown by the partnering work done by World Vision, there also remain questions concerning the sustainability of the partner network, along with the associated project outcomes. This is particularly the case regarding the “interface” between community and partners, on the one hand, and various government services, on the other. It seems that currently an NGO of World Vision’s standing plays a vital role in fostering this engagement, as well as a host of subsidiary partnering arrangements. At the risk of overstating World Vision’s significance, it is not clear, with our limited information, just who could be a similarly trusted, disinterested and connected community leader in its place. This of course creates a distinct difficulty for projects that are conspicuously constructed as ‘pilots’, with specified deadlines and exit strategies. We suggest, in our ‘Recommendations’, this needs to be a greater focus in this project particularly. One approach may be to explore the viability of a financially self-sustaining business modelled on the current World Vision project team, with operating costs provided in part or in whole by advisory fees and government grants, rather than by World Vision itself. Regardless of the mechanism, devolving community leadership in local economic development is an identifiable priority.

One note of criticism regarding the current project design and implementation is that the outputs appear both numerous highly instrumental, particularly in relation to the partnering strategy. For example, the Project Design (World Vision South Africa 2012) identifies Output 1.3 as ‘One Tourism Association and one existing Cleaning and Waste Management Cooperative strengthened through partnerships’. Other outputs are similarly definitive. We met with a wide range of partners – some local community groups, others small business owners, others again involved in child protection and other forms of service provision – and could imagine substantial time involved in basic co- ordination of activities across this range of organisations and industries. We also saw a commensurate range of capacities of partners – some operating at a subsistence level, others with sophisticated organisational structures and practices. Given time and resource constraints on the project team, it is possible that a more focussed approach – working with fewer partners, with perhaps a broader agenda and less prescriptive outputs in each case – would accomplish more. This would entail a much less ambitiously scoped project design and in the process may help reduce the evidentiary burden of reporting and budgetting described by staff above. Alternatively, this may constitute evidence that the project is ready to be scaled up, with more staff put in place to co- ordinate the many partner relationships more intensively.

Discussion of the Coding Scheme Results

While the resulting scheme has significant correspondences with the major themes and questions in the framework, it did manage to highlight some unforeseen areas, including those of interest to urban programming generally. Of the top twenty codes, for example, we expected themes like ‘Employment and Business Opportunity’, ‘Partnering and Networking’, ‘Youth and Children’, ‘World Vision’ and ‘Urban program effectives’ to dominate. More surprising are those areas that were seen to be obstacles to the project goal of economic development, and aim of child wellbeing. ‘Government Services and Engagement’ is chief among these (and is further implicitly referenced in prominent themes of ‘Accountability and Corruption’, ‘Urban Environment and Infrastructure’ and ‘Resourcing’). This reinforces the view that the ‘government’ (at whatever local, state or national level this is construed) is deeply embedded in community and partner consciousness as a critical agent in the improvement of economic and other development outcomes. Currently it is not seen as sufficiently active in the pursuit of these goals.

Other key themes that emerged from the coding exercise show the confluence of macro-level sociological factors in this setting. While we have no benchmark for comparison at this time, we note again that the project objectives, goals and aims here remain tightly interconnected with servicing basic needs in a range of areas. People need further ‘education, skills and training’ to prepare for employability and business development. ‘Information and communication’ channels need to be readily available to capitalise on economic opportunities. Communities need to be cohesive, ‘engaged and cooperative’ in some sense, for individuals to engage in flourishing production and consumption. Cost-effective and reliable ‘mobility and transport’ options need to be available for businesses, employees and consumers to trade effectively. And basic human physical and psychological needs, such as ‘food and housing’, ‘health and wellbeing’, reprieve from ‘poverty’ and ‘security [and] safety’ must be met for industrial productivity to grow.

Again, we emphasise that understanding, much less acting upon, all of these issues is an insurmountable undertaking in a short period. Yet, from a developed urban perspective, we suspect a similar set of interviews would be unlikely to touch upon the same range of fundamental social issues in the context of economic development alone. The coding results – while driven to a degree by the line of questioning – add weight to the argument that World Vision is helping most precisely through its role as a trusted advisor of community and business, and its current orientation towards partnering, facilitation and advocacy is a practical – if somewhat intangible – means towards those broader social ends.

In broad terms, comparing the current review’s recommendations with the baseline reports shows several points, both of similarity and difference, of interest. Firstly, we note some of the same lack of clarity continues with regard to economic development, in particular concerning the relationship to child wellbeing. At a general level, however, we have considerable sympathy with those designing and implementing the project – it is far from clear from the broader development literature exactly ‘what works’ with regard to fostering economic growth in developing regions. Moreover the causal connection between growth and child wellbeing is both highly suggestive at a statistical level (GDP, education and child mortality are all strongly correlated for example), and notoriously hard to demonstrate with smaller sampling frames, due to a host of possible confounds. It is therefore the sort of relationship which is both extremely plausible over the long run, and extremely difficult to show progress with in the short run.

With respect to what can be demonstrated – that corrupt governance, excessive bureaucracy, lack of education, poor physical and informational infrastructure, crime and violence, health epidemics and gender inequality all acts as breaks on business development, wealth creation and employment growth – the project seems to be correctly concentrating its limited resources on “high yield” activities. Numerous community participants spoke of World Vision’s active role in consulting with government authorities, including the police, local and federal authorities. The partner network has grown, in both depth and breadth. And there have been a number of specific activities aimed at improving communication, information, education and skills around business development and employment. We also acknowledge the symbolic importance of World Vision’s presence in the area, as a trusted figure. In the light of complaints about official authorities, the importance of this informal role should not be underestimated.

The project team should consider how it responds to its broad focus – i.e. more staff, better partnering or re-scoping the project.

Review Methodology

We discuss here several aspects of the review methodology. Some aspects of the methodology have been inherited from World Vision’s general DM&E approach; other aspects have been trialled here for the first time.

Interviews

The three interview scripts were prepared in the two weeks leading up to the interviews, and were piloted and refined on the Monday of the interview week. The economic questions devised by David Lansley proved to be easy to ask and explain in most cases, and showed relatively high response rates across the community participants (to whom they were largely directed).

The framework questions were more difficult to ask, particularly those directed towards community participants. More critically, interviews proved to be useful ways of gathering information in relation to certain themes particularly: community members were able to answer questions on themes of “Levels of poverty & Urban Vulnerabilities” and “Governance/ Power structures/ Accountability”, while partners responded to questions of “Partnering/ Networks/ Connections”. World Vision staff interviews covered a broader range of themes (both directly in response to questions, and indirectly through extensive and wide-ranging responses). Our questions though were naturally weighted towards themes that only WV staff could answer: “Partnering/ Networks/ Connections”, “Program Effectiveness” and “Organisational systems & structures”.

Unfortunately two of the URBAN CONTEXT themes, “Diversity” and “Mobility”, were difficult to explore with any of the interview groups, other than in very generic terms. For example, findings show that diversity is growing in Orlando due to both southern migration in Africa and urbanisation of South Africans. Two of the small business operators happened to be from Mozambique; other respondents (confirmed by background literature) discussed growing populations from Zimbabwe and other countries in Soweto generally, which is leading to a greater need for infrastructure and housing. In the case of mobility, responses indicated several ways in which mobility is complicating economic development: as people move back and forth between urban and rural homes; as skilled and wealthier residents move to “the suburbs” (located primarily to the north rather than south of Johannesburg); and, as skilled labourers move from local to city- based locations (working for larger companies). Similarly, lack of mobility was cited as a problem in some cases, due to the time and expense of commuting outside Soweto. However both themes emerged from discussions rather than direct questioning. For these themes, as well as for the theme of “Levels of poverty & Urban Vulnerabilities”, other sources of data are needed to analyse meaningfully any trends. This is discussed further in the Mixed Methods Approach and again in the Recommendations for Review Methodology sections below.

Quantitative Surveys

We included in the surveys a number of questions requiring a response on a scale of 1-10. Given the overall sample of respondents was small, and further broken up into three categories, these are limited use for any statistical purposes (though they may shed light or clarify particular quantitative responses). We suggest, if feasible, that separate survey instruments be designed and administered to a larger sample in future reviews, using the current rating questions as a basis. This would accomplish several aims:

  • Questionnaires are quicker to administer than interviews, and could be administered as a separate process, and even by separate staff
  • Larger samples (at least 20-30) would produce more statistically robust findings
  • Findings could also be compared across pilot sites

The scales themselves also warrant greater refinement and scrutiny, in terms of both wording and the underlying constructs being measured. This is largely dependent upon the future direction of the Urban Meta-Analysis Framework generally; as that matures through further review iterations, the scales should be adjusted and elaborated upon accordingly. As a further point, the current literature on wellbeing and quality-of-life surveying is now both large and reasonably mature; it may be possible to investigate possible concordances between existing surveys and the aims of urban framework, and to pick up existing well-tested scales where appropriate (as indeed has been the case for the “Partnership Assessment Tool”).

Testing the Questions…

As we have noted, this review constituted an opportunity to trial a methodology and framework for understanding the impact of urban development. We had a number of difficulties translating high level concepts of ‘urbanism’ and urban studies to a series of questions that were both relevant and understood by participants. We spent considerable time in the lead-up to, and on the first day of the review testing and refining the questions; even still, in practice, we found certain questions were more relevant and also easier to ask. This was particularly the case, surprisingly, for the World Vision staff interviews. The original staff survey was similar to the partner survey; after administering it to one staff member, we opted in favour of more open-ended and reflective questions reflected in the current survey. These probed the same sorts of themes and questions that are included in the urban meta- analysis framework, without the same level of prescription. As a side note, we felt that the “accidents” of cross-administering surveys (variations on the community survey was used with both a partner – the SMME forum – and a staff member) actually proved productive; however since the responses in both cases tended to confirm the overall trends, it would have been repetitive to continue with the same questions with other partner and staff participants.

Adopting a Mixed Methods Approach

More generally, even in the limited time contexts of the pilot reviews, we would advocate for something of a ‘mixed methods approach’. We note in the Baseline Report that both network mapping and “Walk & Talk” methods were used to supplement the project findings. Both activities could be, we believe, constructively employed during the reviews as well, even in truncated form, as part of the first day orientation for reviewers for example. More critically, they provide other sources of data against which to compare and constrast information given through obstrusive methods such as interviews and questionnaires.

Similarly we suggest future reviews understand as much of the general urban context of a given pilot project as possible – through websites, films and other cultural forms, and academic and other publications. While the reviews generally won’t encompass scope for formal content analysis or ethnographic work, such secondary sources can be used profitably to orient and triangulate particular project findings. Moreover they help identify ‘macro’-social trends that may not be apparent through direct observation in the field. We suggest some particular data sources and methods:

  • Review of available government statistics and secondary literature (this is partially conducted in the current review for instance).

  • Structured observation of the geographical area(s) and social behaviours addressed by the project. This could be particularly helpful with “Urban Context”-type questions concerning Diversity, Mobility and other themes.

  • Anonymous paper or web-based questionnaires.

  • Discourse or content analysis of documentary and media sources (newspapers, blogs, film, books, photos, maps, and other forms of cultural expression)

Exactly how such activities should be conducted would need to be specified and adjusted for each project, but would ideally be factored into the review process ahead of time.

General Comments on Methodology

We also note several aspects of the review methodology that either (a) worked well and so should be adopted or (b) could be improved for future reviews:

  • Interviewers and note-takers should be as familiar as possible with the review objectives and the specific interview questions, as well as general techniques and ethical considerations when conducting interviews. We include the generic instructions in the Instructions for Interviewers appendix used in the Orlando East review as a starting point for a more complete guide in future reviews.

  • For each interview, participants should be given an ethics package, including: a ‘plain language statement’, outlining the purposes of the interview, a consent form detailing how the interview data will be used, and information about who they should contact with any concerns. While participants in the Orlando East context were briefed about these aspects, time constraints often meant details were omitted in practice. Again, the statement and consent form used in the Orlando East included in the Interview Purpose and Consent Form could be used as a template for future reviews.

  • Where practical and appropriate, interviews should be recorded as well as noted. While transcription may often not be feasible, recordings can be used to confirm quotes during the report write-up.

  • In Orlando East, the first afternoon was spent ‘role-playing’ interviews, with different people playing note-taker, interviewer and interviewee. We recommend this be adopted in future reviews, as it assisted us in identifying difficult- to-ask questions, and also introduced the review staff to the note-takers prior to actual interviews being conducted.

  • Review staff should be briefed in the weeks leading up to the review about the project, interview schedule and participants. This was helpful in the Orlando East context; however, more details, particularly regarding the participants, would have allowed staff to be more precise in terms of the direction and focus of questions.

  • As noted above, review staff should have some practical orientation (through relatively informal ‘Walk & Talk’ and network mapping activities) prior to commencing interviews. Arriving one or two days ahead of time (e.g. on the preceding Friday or Saturday) would help with this.

On the whole, the review process proved productive and certainly elicited critical insights and information. However we anticipate future reviews will benefit from a certain level of methodological pilotting undertaken in the Orlando East project.

Development in the Urban Context

Given the economic focus of our questions, it is also not surprising that the themes of poverty, unemployment, resourcing and transport are raised frequently. This is evident both in the direct findings, and in our coding of responses, where ‘Employment and Business Opportunity’ appears with greatest frequency (191 occurrences). However the diversity of our respondents also brought out the complexity and interconnections of issues facing the community – and pointed to a general difficulty of formulating succinct and measurable ‘theories of change’ in an urban environment. A particular example of this came out in discussion with the project manager of a child protection NGO operating in the area. She described how many of the children they cared for had been orphaned by parents dying of HIV/AIDS. As a consequence, these children lived with grandparents (particularly grandmothers), who often are past working age, living on pensions and are less connected with patterns of modern work life. Upon reaching adolescence, such children are faced with severely limited local employment opportunities and a lack of home-based vocational training. Gangs, crime and drugs provide natural avenues to absorb this excess labour capacity. In some cases, young women will contract HIV/AIDS intentionally, to apply for government support grants, and in the process, pertuating a vicious cycle. This particular anecdote highlights a number of themes – HIV/AIDS, drugs, crime, poverty, unemployment, child wellbeing – as well as, importantly, their interdependence.

Such complex networks of factors clearly complicate the development agenda. Development projects naturally need a focus – arguably the focus of this project, ‘local economic development’, is already too broad given the resources on the ground. The theories of change posed both in the Baseline Report (World Vision South Africa 2010a) and Project Design (World Vision South Africa 2012) documents, for example, posit a close causal connection between child wellbeing (‘Project Aim’) and economic development (‘Project Goal’). On the one hand, it is not clear that this causal link is justified in anything other than at an extremely high national level (where economic indicators have a strong correlation with general human development indicators for example). On the other hand, it is also clear that in the Orlando East context, numerous other factors are also integral. As the above anecdote indicates, both child wellbeing and economic development outcomes benefit from lower HIV/AIDS contraction rates, lower crime, better government accountability and greater education options. Where are interventions best directed?

This reflects a more general issue that has received considerable attention in the development literature of late: “What works?” In urban contexts, this question is accentuated. Cities represent spatial configurations with high (though uneven) human density, mobility, opportunity, diversity, connectivity and levels of affiliation and relationships. Child well-being is accordingly impacted with greater intensity by dynamic and complex combinations of health, crime, employment, politics, transport, education and technology factors. Only in the most desparate and immediate circumstances will physical infrastructure (such as the example of the “well” mentioned by a staff member) be relevant in this context. Even then, such solutions are naturally costly to scale.

In some respects the challenge of urban development programming seems to be one of accelerating the ‘natural’ evolution towards the kinds of post- industrial democratic, relatively affluent and cosmopolitan cities that have achieved the prized status of ‘liveability’. As the nineteenth and twentieth century histories of cities such as London, Paris and Melbourne show, this is a hard-fought (and frequently non-linear) evolution. Nor, as numerous cities show, is such progress inevitable. While prescriptive recipes for successful cities have been difficult to identify (and moreover are usually overdetermined by political ideology), some of the controllable obstacles to development are more evident. A number of these indeed appear emphatically in our findings:

  • Health impacts of manageable or eradicable disease
  • Lack of education – particularly in areas of financial literacy, project and business management
  • Government bureaucracy, “red tape”, inaccessability and corruption
  • Lack of co-ordination between community, business and government sectors
  • Failure to identify and exploit economic comparative advantage
  • Environmental hazards
  • Inefficiencies brought about by lack of infrastructure and services
  • High social and economic inequity

Focussing on negatives may work against the impulse of ‘transformational development’ espoused by NGOs like World Vision. Arguably they translate into more naturally into the promotion of ‘positive freedoms’ in an urban community context however. In the context of economic development, it is difficult for an NGO to advise communities how to generate profitable businesses for example. By discovering and endevouring to address what artificial limits are obstructing business and employment opportunities, individuals can at least experiment with what options the free market has to offer. For Orlando East, much of the practical work appears to be focussed precisely in this direction. We tentatively suggest that DM&E activities be aligned; for example, by choosing indicators that reflect the removal of the kinds of systemic obstacles discussed above, rather than comparatively simple but unrepresentative measures such as the number of individuals who have been trained, found employment, founded businesses, and so on. While this might result in less ‘good news’ stories to tell, it would more correctly relate much of the important work World Vision had been able to achieve in this project.

Towards this end, we now discuss the Urban Meta-Analysis Framework (UMF), put together by the Centre of Expertise in Urban Programming to guide DM&E in the urban context.

Conclusions and Recommendations

Conclusions

The Orlando East Project encompasses a broad range of economic development activities, centred on partnering, facilitation and advocacy. Given the challenges of the Soweto and South African environment, this is both a notable and complex undertaking. Our findings show:

  • Orlando East is a highly complex urban environment. As part of Soweto, it is facing a swelling population, particularly due to sub-Saharan and internal migration. It also continues to experience high rates of HIV/AIDS, crime, child abuse, government corruption, skills and service shortages and general poverty. Each of these is closely linked to the larger World Vision aim of improving child wellbeing.

  • Economic development is very uneven in Orlando East. While there are budding areas of entrepreneurialism and more established markets in tourism, construction and in various informal economic sectors, many community members still survive on substistence incomes. This economic inequality contributes to broader social problems, and certainly affects child wellbeing outcomes.

  • Community members (and to a lesser degree, partners and WV staff) were critical of both local and national government’s lack of action in promoting economic development. As a priority, increasing information, skills and education (particularly in financial literacy) are critical to developing business and employment opportunities.

  • World Vision is a highly regarded ‘actor’ in the community. This is testament not only to the organisation as a whole, but to the specific individuals involved in the project. The main recommendation from review participants is that World Vision scale up its involvement both in Orlando East and elsewhere in Soweto and South Africa.

  • The UMF framework and methodology proved useful in extracting many of the complexities about the environment and the project. Both can be productively enhanced in future reviews. In the Orlando East context, for example, improvements include collecting data:
  • from other parts of Soweto to better understand the unique characteristics of Orlando East

  • and from other parts of Johannesburg to better understand the interaction of Soweto with the South Africa’s dominant financial and commercial centre.

Recommendations for the Orlando East Project

Our main concern is a level of disconnection between the ambitions of the project design and the practical resources on the ground. We suggest some possible areas for the project team to consider:

  • A more targetted and strategic approach to partnering. We met with a number of partners, and no doubt many more organisations have formal or informal relationships with World Vision in the area. Careful assessment of the strengths and contributions of actual or prospective partners would increase the chances of greater program impact. This may result in greater involvement with partners, such as the SMME Forum and PlanAct, that are more directly involved in economic development activities, and also have strong organisational capacities for facilitating this. Project outputs could also be simplified to focus on what can be achieved through stronger – and possibly financial – partnerships with these kinds of organisations.

  • Related to this, we suggest possibly separating general World Vision community assistance from its economic development work. Current staff seemed adept at separating these activities (and as we note, in practice they are closely related in this context). However they do not seem to be formally acknowledged, and complicate the project design.

  • As we note, economic development is a highly complex field, and even more so given the other challenges in this particular environment. We recommend that operational staff be supplemented by research capacity in the areas of business, finance and economics – possibly through internships from the University of Johannesburg. This could assist in identifying particular opportunities for business and employment in the area.

  • We also note the need of a more regular flow of information about programs, and consequently the need to increase staff capacity to collect information on a regular basis between larger reviews. Additional research capacity

  • The aim of creating a self-sustaining partner network in the area is, in our view, currently unrealistic. We suggest this either means extending the life of the pilot considerably, or examining mechansisms, such as a consultancy or small non-government organisation, to take up the work currently undertaken by World Vision. The latter option is unlikely to be productive without at least some kind of ongoing support from World Vision itself.

  • The link between economic development and child wellbeing is difficult to demonstrate, and to measure, in any kind of realistic project 3-5 year timeframe. This poses the question of whether a longer term plan needs to be defined, at least schematically. Such a plan could include transitional arrangements, where specific World Vision-funded development projects could ‘spin-off’ to other entities. Currently it is difficult to see who those entities might be in this area.

  • Staff expressed concerns about the time and complexity of current processes for reporting and budgeting. Given the nature of the work on this project, these concerns appeared well founded. We recommend finding ways to allow for more flexible allocation of funds to support ‘soft’ development work involved in partnering, facilitation and advocacy (covering various areas such as venue hire, catering, transportation, communication, printing and so on).

  • Associated with the previous point, it may be that current project management tools need to be modified to fit the purpose and mode of engagement in the community. Moving forward, a key contribution of this pilot could be the trialling of differential approaches to M&E/financial management that reflect the elements of facilitation, negotiation, relationship-building, and other associated activities.

Recommendations for the Review Methodology

We make the following recommendations for the methodological aspects of future reviews:

  • Continue with iterations of the current community, partner and staff instruments (including the Partner Assessment Tool).

  • Incorporate formal instructions for interviewers, plain language statements and consent forms.

  • Continue with the practice of ‘role-playing’ interviews on the first day of the review.

  • Record interviews (and assess the usefulness of speech recognition software to convert interviews to text).

  • Separate ‘quant’ from ‘qual’ survey instruments, and conduct the quantitative surveys separately, with larger cohorts.

  • Terms like “community”, “government”, “partnership” and “project” should be anchored at the outset of the interview (as well as clarified as much as possible at the start of the review process). In practice we often clarified these terms, but not always in consistent ways. A recommendation would be to have: A specific set of questions that asks the respondents to name their “community”, their interaction with “government”, the nature of their “partnership” with World Vision, and their knowledge of and interaction with the “project”.

  • Include additional methods where appropriate, including:
    • Network mapping
    • Semi-structured observation (using “Walk&Talk” or similar approaches)
    • Analysis of secondary materials, including published literature, statistical sources and urban cultural forms (both before and after conducting field research)

  • Continue to examine methods for measuring impact and effectiveness of partnering, facilitation and advocacy. One specific recommendation here is to develop an indicator of partner network ‘maturity’–the extent to which a set of partnerships could survive without one or more participants.

Recommendations for the Urban Meta-analysis Framework

While the framework as a whole provides a relevant spectrum of themes, questions and indicators, we recommend a broadening of the areas discussed in the area of Urban Themes particularly. This would enable a more flexibile theoretical instrument to guide the selection and adaptation of specific indicators and questions for reviews.

We also recommend ongoing efforts to map broad trends in cities and urban literature to a coherent urban model. This would provide a means also for tying high level World Vision constructs such as the ‘Cities for Children’ through to specific review and evaluation instruments.

Introduction

This Urban Discovery Project (UDP) began in February 2008, with financial and technical support from World Vision Australia (WVA) and the Urban Programming Initiative (UPI)[@wvc2010baseline]. It is due to be completed in September, 2013.

This project is one of six such discovery projects; the other five are located in:

  • Orlando East, Johannesburg, South Africa
  • Surabaya, Indonesia
  • ??, India
  • Beirut, Lebanon
  • La Paz, Bolivia

All six projects have two key features that distinguish them for other WV projects: they are focused on urban areas, and they are issue-based. The six projects are being evaluated in 2012/13 as part of a ‘meta-evaluation’, seeking to address the first of these features: what makes cities different, as sites for aid and development assistance? This report is the second to be written as part of the meta-evaluation (the first report examines Orlando East). Part of it furthers a response to this question.

The evaluation also addresses the particular issue of this project, the question of land security.

Land Ownership in Cambodia

Land is a concern in many developing contexts, due in part to the strong competing forces for land use. In rural settings, land contestation is frequently over natural resources. A recent WV evaluation of a land-based project, fortuitously also based in Cambodia, suggests land issues arise ‘as government owned state forest is being allocated for development to companies investing in cash crops, mining and agriculture’ [@wvc2012eval].

In an urban context, land is valuable more due to the competing claims of diverse city inhabitants. These include business developers, keen on speculative investment; government departments of varied stripes (from local to national, and across different ministries and concerns); and urban communities, families, workers and residents, each with specific land interests and needs. Land use, accordingly, is also more diverse, with space needed for housing, places of work, transportation, recreation and commerce, as well as large-scale utility infrastructure for managing large populations: water provision, sewage, energy generation, waste. In this respect, there is arguably greater similarity between rich and poor cities, than poor cities and their surrounding rural environments. One key difference we focus on here is how land disputes are managed and resolved.

The land question in Cambodia and Phnom Penh is especially complicated. Recent history is dominated by accounts of the 3 and a half year rule of the Khmer Rouge. In 1975 Phnom Penh was forcibly emptied, and land - both urban and rural - was appropriated by the state under a policy of brutal and near total agricultural collectivisation [cite]. In 1979, after the fall of the regime, people returned to Phnom Penh to reclaim, among other things, their property. In the decades since, Cambodia has seen communism give way to Vietnam-led socialism in the 80’s, followed by progressive - if slow - movement towards liberal capitalism and integration into the broader global economic and political community. Much of this transition has taken place under a nominal democracy, in practice presided over by the world’s longest serving prime minister, Hun Sen.

Land reform has become a centerpiece of Sen’s government in the 2000’s.

The evaluation followed a qualitative approach, comprising mainly of a series of informant interviews with community members, government officials, partner NGOs and World Vision staff. The evaluation was conducted by three WVA staff and one external consultant from RMIT University. Remy Herbert represented the UPI group; Julie Smith has had extensive experience with other World Vision Cambodian projects; and Nicki ? has a general background in legal and aid issues. Conclusions and Recommendations

Conclusions

Overall, our team’s conclusions broadly align from those of the baseline report [@wvc2010baseline]. The report authors claimed the UDP:

  • had been successful in empowering ‘communities to lead their own development and increase tenure security’, but, as noted by partners, there are ‘limits to the successes of the UDP… as the forces arrayed against it are too numerous’.

  • had also successfully built relationships with other NGOs and with government, particularly at the Sangkat level.

  • had worked extremely well as an innovative learning ‘cell’ within World Vision.

  • needs greater support from ‘World Vision systems’, which ‘run contrary to the work that UDP is conducting’.

Three years later, these same observations remain true. However there are other observations that could now be added to the mix on the basis of our findings.

Given the state of the UDP project, our recommendations also depart significantly from the baseline report. Principally they differ in terms of generality: there seems little point to specific programmatic recommendations, whereas the broader questions of how to leverage the UDP “experience” in the current Cambodian seems highly relevant.

Urban Context

  • The issue of land rights is a national one. Currently efforts to reform land rights are underway, in response to widespread dissatisfaction among both urban and rural populations. However Phnom Penh forms only approximately 15% of the Cambodian population, while the question of land, as discussed above, is considerably more contentious. Current efforts to register land are therefore focussed on rural land rights - both a larger voting bloc to appease, and presumably, concerns that are easier to remedy. This politically-informed choice is probably the single biggest obstacle to the resolution of land registration for the 15 urban communities involved in this project.

  • The project is scheduled for completion in late 2013; however the land issue will not be resolved for these communities. While the communities appear to have taken on a degree of autonomous communication - with eachother, with government and with other partners - World Vision remains a central participant in this network. This raises a broader question (also evident in Orlando East) about how UDPs plan for and execute an effective exit strategy. Difficult to quantify, at least some of the gains would be lost if the UDP wound up for good at this stage.

  • From many of the interviews, there was a clear expectation from communities that the government would eventually resolve the land issue - though not necessarily in their favour. It is less clear what the ‘Plan B’ might be: would these communities simply dissipate? Would they be sufficiently compensated, and on what basis? Could the community survive, even if transplanted to another location? What provision would be made for employment, education and other service delivery in that event?

  • The UDP is rightly focused on the needs of the communities it is assisting. However Phnom Penh has many other stakeholders with at least indirectly competing needs. Even partial exposure to the project made the evaluation team acutely aware of heavy economic and social activity in areas immediately approximate to the project communities. Over time, competition for land

  • in the form of higher rents and lower availability - will inevitably increase.

  • Cambodia’s economy has been growing steadily, with GDP per capita (PPP) growing at an average of 9% per annum for the past decade. This is largely fueled by garment and natural resource exports, with a smaller slice made up by tourism. A tentative conclusion based on growth in manufacturing and service sectors is that the current urban composition (15%) will also grow. As in other east Asian cities, this is only likely to exacerbate pressures on existing urban land usage. It is likely that the most disadvantaged under these conditions will not be relatively settled and secure communities, but new urban poor workers looking for opportunities on the fringes of Phnom Penh. These of course could include members of resettled communities targeted by this project, alongside rural migrants.

Recommendations

  • Land rights in Phnom Penh is likely to be an ongoing issue, both for these communities and incoming migrants. The UDP has developed important skills and relationships. These should be used to help address land and other emerging issues, either through a modified ongoing UDP project, or as part of more general ADP work in Phnom Penh.

  • There are signs that as Cambodia’s economy grows in manufacturing and service sectors, Phnom Penh, as the only large-scale urban settlement, will bear the brunt of internal economic migration. Similarly the land reforms will eventually migrate to Phnom Penh, creating resolution in some cases, and potentially confusion and dislocation in others. The UDP offers a organisational vehicle where city-wide impacts of broader economic and political change can be monitored and addressed.

  • In the event that the UDP continues, we suggest a greater focus on developing - through partnerships or recruitment - legal and urban planning expertise, as well as access to more and differentiated levels of government and business.

  • The urban context itself is somewhat underplayed in available project documents. The urban location provides both positives and negatives: greater access to different levels of government and diverse partners, for example, but also a greater number of public and private interests to satisfy, as well as (obviously) higher density in and of itself creating higher demand for land. Given the “pilot” status of this project, greater attention to its “urban” character would help outside parties (including evaluators) understand how it differs (and needs to differ) from other development projects.

  • Specifically, the UDP should engage more directly with those actors who have other uses for the land where these communities reside. This would provide both more realistic expectations for the communities themselves, and offer further avenues for advocacy.

Enabling Environment

  • Despite economic growth, gradual democratic form and increased global engagement, Cambodia still rates very poorly in terms of transparency.1 Even in the most transparent of government regimes, land and urban planning sees relatively high levels of corruption at local and state levels of government. It is not surprising therefore that we received anecdotal evidence that corruption plays a major role in settling land issues in Phnom Penh. Relations with government - at all levels - need to be handled extremely sensitively in light of this.

  • We also note the importance of a major international NGO contributing to this controversial and political issue. World Vision’s very presence - almost irrespective of its effectiveness - provides an important counterweight to the strong commercial and political interests invested in land issues.

  • A clear difficulty of the project is the dependency upon government actions. Greater certainty over the time lines the government has forecast for resolving urban land rights would reduce uncertainty among the communities.

  • There are more than 400 NGOs operating in Cambodia as a whole. With a project as large and diffuse as urban land rights, partnering is vital: to make sure that different NGO strengths are used effectively, and to minimise conflict and duplication of effort. Project partners were broadly complimentary of World Vision’s efforts in this regard. However some felt World Vision lacked the flexibility and expertise to address the complexities of land rights effectively.

  • Specifically, two smaller NGO partners - CMDP and UPWD - are of critical importance to the project’s ongoing viability.

  • Much of the work of the UDP focuses on “three planks” of partnering, facilitation and advocacy. The actual work of staff appeared far more focused on partnering and facilitation. While the government officials we met with expressed a high regard for World Vision’s work, it wasn’t clear what actual advocacy work had been planned or carried out. “Soft advocacy” appears to be indirect, through the agency of the communities.

Recommendations

  • From the literature, “soft advocacy” appears a relatively ‘soft’ concept, capable of many interpretations. Given its significance to both this and other ADPs, we recommend a more direct focus on practical advocacy work training, guidelines and methods of monitoring performance. The UDP network within World Vision - as well as strategic NGO partners with expertise in this area - could help develop such guidelines.

  • Government relations in Cambodia are complex, and require specific skills. Aside from the project leader, it is unclear who in the UDP (or elsewhere in the organisation) might provide those skills. More attention to higher level advocacy skills is recommended to address this.

  • Similarly, it has not always been clear what soft advocacy activity is for. At this stage, one feasible outcome would be definitive timelines on when the government plans to resolve urban land rights. This could drive advocacy over the remaining months of the project, and help provide greater clarity for communities to plan for future advocacy work on their own behalf.

  • World Vision should maintain a visible (if less resourced) presence in this project, for at least 2-3 years after project completion. There are several possible models for this:

    • a continuation of the UDP itself, for a shorter time period and with a smaller team.
    • incorporation (“mainstreaming”) into ADP programming
    • secondment of 1-2 staff to other partners with direct project involvement (CMDP or UPWD)

  • More time, at this stage, should be directed towards helping communities consider and plan for alternative scenarios, in the event land is not granted.

  • Key project partners (CMDP and UPWD) should be briefed on the plans for winding down or continuing the UDP in modified form.

Organisational Context

  • Partners, government officials, and internal UDP and ADP staff all spoke very generally of the benefits the UDP had delivered to World Vision Cambodia as a whole. Quite independently of the specific benefits of the project’s urban and land issue focus, having models that encourage programming and procedural experimentation and innovation appears to be highly beneficial.

  • The urban focus of this project was hard for both World Vision staff and the evaluation to articulate. This is in part due to the fact that “urban” is partly synonymous with the issue focus of this (and other) UDPs. As one of the evaluation team put it, there is relatively little difference between the actual activities of this urban project, compared with other issue-based projects (urban or otherwise). That is perhaps not surprising given the issue of land is pertinent to both urban and rural settings (particularly in Cambodia at this time).

  • Several of the partners felt World Vision is somewhat rigid as an organisation, particularly in relation to expenditure and allocation of responsibilities to project activities. This is a concern in a dynamic and fast-changing urban context especially.

  • Much of the purported work of the UDP focusses on “three planks” of partnering, facilitation and advocacy. The actual work of staff appeared far more focussed on partnering and facilitation. While the government officials we met with expressed a high regard for World Vision’s work, it wasn’t clear what actual advocacy work had been planned or carried out. “Soft advocacy” appears to be indirect, through the agency of the communities.

  • Methodologically, the mix of World Vision and external staff proved useful. However it would be useful to clarify - for all members of the evaluation team - what is meant by evaluation in the UDP pilot project context. It is clear for instance that currently the UDP projects (in both Orlando East and Phnom Penh) are difficult to evaluate in ordinary terms, in spite of the presence of baseline reports. This is for several reasons:

    • the six different projects focus on different issues, with different resourcing levels and very different social, economic and political contexts. Comparing across these contexts is extremely difficult, without at least (a) a similar project design and (b) similar resourcing levels.

    • without quantitative indicators, it is also difficult to evaluate change over time in a given project context. For example, we have similar conclusions to the 2010 baseline report, but not a clear sense of what exact progress has been made since then. Consequently it is also difficult to evaluate the project over time.

    • the project is one of six UDP pilots. Evaluation in that context means, arguably, something more qualitative, constructivist and exploratory than what would usually be understood by the term.

Recommendations

  • Future UDP projects should be more focused, both in terms of goals / outcomes / outputs and, more importantly, the steps for implementation. This is particularly important given the social character of this kind of work (partnering, facilitation, advocacy, etc). Though difficult, some concrete guidelines and indicators could help bind these activities conceptually to project outcomes and outputs.

  • Related to the previous recommendation: both the Orlando East and Phnom Penh cases suffer something of a disconnect between the “high level” aspirational language of baseline and project design reports, and what appears to be the concrete work done by project teams. This is natural given the pilot nature of both cases. However future UDP projects would benefit from a more streamlined level of connectivity between various World Vision frameworks and processes (child wellbeing indicators, LEAP language), and project-specific activities.

  • UDP projects should have smaller timeframes and more flexible allocations of resources.

  • Risk management needs greater refinement. The project goal is heavily dependent upon government action, which is naturally subject to other influences. Adverse government actions, in particular, need specific mitigation strategies.

  • For future UDPs, evaluation should be both more frequent (perhaps annual) and utilise a greater range of mixed methods, to get a greater diversity of data on key indicators (once the indicators themselves are clearer).
